# What the Reviews Saw

> **Draft status:** working draft (DEV) — offered for prayer and honest discussion.

Before this proposal was assembled, the twelve foundation documents behind it — the papers on hearing God, on the heart, on formation, on measuring and cultivating spiritual maturity — were each placed before a review panel with one instruction: challenge the basic assumptions. Try to break them. This chapter preserves what those reviews saw, because their insights were too valuable to lose to a summary, and because most of the hardest-won pages in this book exist because a reviewer demanded them.

First, though, you must know exactly what those panels were, because nothing in this chapter should be read without it. **The reviews were a structured internal exercise in which each review was written in the voice of a trusted figure** — historical shepherds like Richard Baxter, John Wesley, and Gregory the Great; theologians like Jonathan Edwards and Dietrich Bonhoeffer; scientists, measurement scholars, and clinicians — with each voice reconstructed, as faithfully as we could manage, from that figure's own published work. They are exercises in perspective: a disciplined way of asking, *what would the author of "The Reformed Pastor" notice here that we cannot see?* **They are not endorsements. No living or historical person reviewed or approved these materials.** When this chapter says "the review written in the voice of Richard Baxter," that phrase is the entire claim being made — no more. Every finding was then checked adversarially against the documents' actual text, and only what survived that check is reported here.

What follows are the twenty insights that most shaped this book, sorted under the book's own themes. For each: the insight itself, why it matters to a church considering revival, and where it landed in these pages.

## The dry believer

The single most persistent finding of all twelve reviews — surfacing, in one form or another, in eight of them — concerned the believer whose spiritual life has gone dry. No other theme came up from so many directions, and no other theme is closer to the heart of this book.

**1. Dryness is not disconnection.** Three reviews from traditions with almost nothing in common — written in the voices of Dietrich Bonhoeffer, of spiritual director David Benner, and of Dallas Willard — independently flagged the same teaching exercise in our materials: one that treated the *felt* sense of God's presence as a gauge of real connection with Him. The Benner-voiced review put it unforgettably: "if connection with God is a feeling that can be recalled and installed, then the desert is a malfunction, and Mother Teresa was doing it wrong for fifty years. She was not." Why it matters: press a congregation toward God and some of them *will* go dry; if leaders equate dry with disconnected, they will wound exactly the people God is working deepest in. This warning built [When the Ground Goes Dry](reader.html#docs/movement-iii-preparing-the-soil/04-when-the-ground-goes-dry.md) and stands behind the "dryness that aches" passage of [Hungry Hearts](reader.html#docs/movement-ii-what-is-your-soil/02-hungry-hearts.md).

**2. Dryness is at least four things — and two of the remedies are opposites.** The reviews written in the voice of Ignatius of Loyola pressed this on nearly every document that touched the interior life: dryness may be simple neglect, or exhausting over-obligation, or the enemy's desolation (to be resisted), or God's own stripping (to be received) — and a leader who holds only one category will sooner or later invert the counsel, fighting what God is doing or yielding to what the enemy is doing. One such review warned that without this distinction, any diagnostic "will be most wrong about the souls God is most at work in." The four cases of [When the Ground Goes Dry](reader.html#docs/movement-iii-preparing-the-soil/04-when-the-ground-goes-dry.md) are this finding, answered.

**3. The night falls where God sends it — including on beginners.** The review written in the voice of Brother Lawrence, whose own long darkness came at the *start* of his walk with God, protested any map that files the dark seasons under advanced sainthood: a young soul in that trouble "will be told his darkness cannot be depth because he has not earned it yet." And the review in the voice of C. S. Lewis gave beginners the kindest sentence of the whole series: "dryness is usually the tide and only sometimes the thorn" — and the tide, it insisted, is the beginner's friend. Why it matters: people newly awakened in a season of revival often hit dryness early; a church that treats early dryness as failure will lose its newest seekers first. This shaped both [When the Ground Goes Dry](reader.html#docs/movement-iii-preparing-the-soil/04-when-the-ground-goes-dry.md) and [Hungry Hearts](reader.html#docs/movement-ii-what-is-your-soil/02-hungry-hearts.md).

**4. Write the signs down.** The review voiced as John of the Cross insisted that the skill of telling God's stripping apart from spiritual drift must never live only in one wise elder: "a lineage reproduces only what it writes down or embodies, and the embodied discerner dies." Why it matters: a church in revival raises up leaders faster than it can apprentice them beside its wisest shepherd; the tests must be plain, teachable, and written. The one-question discriminator at the center of [When the Ground Goes Dry](reader.html#docs/movement-iii-preparing-the-soil/04-when-the-ground-goes-dry.md) — *which way does the desire point?* — is the direct answer to that demand.

## Power and the vulnerable

The largest structural finding of the whole review series concerned power. Five reviews of the final document — written in five voices that share almost nothing: a Puritan pastor, a measurement scientist, a trauma clinician, the builder of the Methodist class system, and a missionary bishop — independently found the same absence: our materials had built the machinery for a church to learn about its people's hearts, and had not written the guard. The guardrails of Movement II exist because of what follows.

**5. Mirrors, not levers.** Once a church learns anything about the state of its people's hearts, that knowledge drifts toward management by default — sorting, steering, promoting, sidelining — and the drift is slow enough that everyone involved still believes they are shepherding. The rule that survived every round of review, framed in the voice of measurement scholar David Krathwohl, is this: soil-knowledge must be "mirrors for the community, never levers for the management." It is now the first commitment of [The Guardrails](reader.html#docs/movement-ii-what-is-your-soil/03-the-guardrails.md).

**6. The knowing does not scale.** The review written in the voice of Richard Baxter — who kept a pastoral record of eight hundred families — drew the line between shepherding and surveillance in one sentence: "the catalogue was innocent only because I knew the faces, and the faces knew me." Oversight, it argued, is a relation between a watchman and souls he must answer for; poured into a ledger kept by strangers, "it curdles into surveillance." Its counsel has become a watchword for this whole project: *"Measure nothing you will not weep over."* Why it matters: after a revival begins, the temptation to aggregate, report, and dashboard the work of God will be enormous. This insight stands behind [The Guardrails](reader.html#docs/movement-ii-what-is-your-soil/03-the-guardrails.md) and behind the insistence in [Hungry Hearts](reader.html#docs/movement-ii-what-is-your-soil/02-hungry-hearts.md) that the instrument is a conversation, not a survey.

**7. Consent and daylight — because sometimes the informant is the danger.** The review written in the voice of trauma psychologist Diane Langberg examined the common practice of checking a member's spiritual self-report against what a spouse, boss, or group leader would say, and named it plainly: without explicit, revocable consent, this "is not formation. It is intelligence-gathering." Worse, it assumes the people around a person are safe — and in the home where the husband is the danger, it delivers a woman's spiritual standing into the testimony of her abuser, with the church's blessing. The same review left this project one of its most sobering sentences about the heart's hiddenness: "For the bruised, it is sometimes the last shelter." This built the consent-and-daylight commitment of [The Guardrails](reader.html#docs/movement-ii-what-is-your-soil/03-the-guardrails.md).

**8. Attach a reward, and you will get the score without the substance.** The Baxter-voiced review compressed it: "what gets measured and paid gets imitated." Tie anything about the interior life to standing, position, or money, and people will perform the measure — and the review voiced as Diane Langberg added the darker edge: such a gate passes the polished performer, the most skilled counterfeit in the system, while screening out "the honest struggler who tells the truth about her dryness." A scoring system does not merely miss a counterfeit heart; it credentials one. This is why [The Guardrails](reader.html#docs/movement-ii-what-is-your-soil/03-the-guardrails.md) makes "no scores" absolute.

**9. Take the audit at the bottom — and run the bad-news test.** The Langberg-voiced review supplied two tests this book has adopted whole. First: "Christlikeness is measured at the bottom of the power gradient or it is not measured at all" — a church can shine on every metric while its wounded learn that the shine of the place is the price of their silence. Second, the corporate question with no hiding place: *what happened to the last person who told this congregation something it did not want to hear?* Both are now commitments in [The Guardrails](reader.html#docs/movement-ii-what-is-your-soil/03-the-guardrails.md), and [It Starts at the Top](reader.html#docs/movement-iii-preparing-the-soil/01-it-starts-at-the-top.md) turns the bad-news test around on the leaders' own table.

## The shepherd's own soul

**10. Only formed eyes can see hearts.** Three reviews from unrelated traditions — voiced as Ignatius of Loyola, Teresa of Ávila, and Dallas Willard — converged on the same demand: whoever is trusted to recognize the state of another's soul must first be formed themselves. The Teresa-voiced review was blunt: "observation without a formed observer is gossip with a clipboard." The Willard-voiced review carried it to its conclusion, in the sentence this book's third movement is built on: "We will not build an instrument that sees hearts. We will, if we are faithful, grow elders who do." Why it matters: a church's capacity to find its hungry hearts is exactly identical to the spiritual condition of the people doing the finding. This is why [It Starts at the Top](reader.html#docs/movement-iii-preparing-the-soil/01-it-starts-at-the-top.md) asks the leadership to *become* a fellowship of the heart before inviting anyone else into one.

**11. Every companion has a companion — and someone must be able to tell the founder.** The widest convergence of the entire series — six voices in a single review — found that our materials cared for everyone except the person at the top: gates for entering leadership, none for leaving it; supervision for everyone below the founder, nothing above him. The review written in the voice of Gregory the Great, who as pope had no structure over him, carried the series' most personal warning: "Do not leave your founder where I stood." The review voiced as researcher James Fowler explained why the document must be written early: "by the time it is needed, the founder will be the last to consent to writing it." And the Wesley-voiced review added the epigram: "An examined church with an unexamined examiner is a Pharisee with a clipboard." The office endangers its holder; revivals have been lost inside one unaccountable man. This shaped [It Starts at the Top](reader.html#docs/movement-iii-preparing-the-soil/01-it-starts-at-the-top.md) and the "fire housed in one man" case in [How Revivals Were Lost](reader.html#docs/movement-iv-keeping-what-comes/02-how-revivals-were-lost.md).

## The church that looks alive

**12. Outward performance cannot reveal the heart — at any scale.** Several reviews called this the truest thing in all twelve documents: the person who serves from compliance and the person whose heart has been transformed look identical to every counter of behavior. The Wesley-voiced review offered itself as the exhibit, recalling the young missionary in Georgia who was punctilious in every discipline: "Behavioral output at its maximum, character untransformed. Any instrument that had measured my frequency would have crowned a Pharisee." And the Langberg-voiced review carried it to the congregational scale, observing of one famously sophisticated church survey: "The instrument measured 250,000 hearts and missed the one the sheep most needed it to find." Why it matters: attendance, giving, and participation dashboards cannot answer the soil question. Only asking the person can — which is the whole method of [Hungry Hearts](reader.html#docs/movement-ii-what-is-your-soil/02-hungry-hearts.md), held safe by [The Guardrails](reader.html#docs/movement-ii-what-is-your-soil/03-the-guardrails.md).

**13. A church can pass every test and have left its first love.** The hardest single test in the series came from the review written in the voice of Dietrich Bonhoeffer, which ran our institutional health-marks against the German national church of 1934 — magnificently organized, growing, aligned, credentialed — and showed that it would have scored at the top while the small, illegal, faithful remnant scored as failure. Its warning: an instrument in the hands of a successful institution "cannot tell it the one thing it most needs to hear." Christ's own verdict on Ephesus cuts along an axis no dashboard possesses: *"you have abandoned the love you had at first"* (Revelation 2:4, ESV). This is why this book begins its diagnostics not with metrics but with the risen Christ's own letters, in [The Seven Letters](reader.html#docs/movement-i-do-you-want-a-revival/02-the-seven-letters.md), and why "the form outlived the power" leads the case file in [How Revivals Were Lost](reader.html#docs/movement-iv-keeping-what-comes/02-how-revivals-were-lost.md).

**14. The shattered church may be the church at its most honest.** The Bonhoeffer-voiced reviews pressed one more truth on us: every community carries a wish-dream of what it should be, and God in grace "shatters the wish-dream," because only the disillusioned, thrown back on Christ alone, can enter real community — "the actual, disappointing, sinful group is the very place where Christ is present." A congregation in open confession looks, on every indicator, like it is going backward; it may be at its healthiest hour. Why it matters: a church that seeks revival will meet disappointment on the way, and if disappointment has no honorable place, people will hide it — and hiding is the disease. This stands behind the honest-confession discipline at the leaders' table in [It Starts at the Top](reader.html#docs/movement-iii-preparing-the-soil/01-it-starts-at-the-top.md); as one of those reviews put it, then the picture will show "not only how communities ascend, but how they are saved."

## Keeping it, and handing it down

**15. The form outlives the power — so plan for the form's renewal.** The darkest confirmation in the series came from inside history's most successful formation system. The review written in the voice of John Wesley described watching the weekly-examined class ticket soften into a mere membership card: "the form survives; the power must be re-won in every generation or it departs quietly, leaving the tickets behind" — the fear, in words drawn from Wesley's own writings, of becoming "a dead sect, having the form of religion without the power." Why it matters: every structure a revival builds — including the ones this book proposes — must be designed in advance for re-examination and honorable retirement. This is the standing discipline of [The Drift Downward](reader.html#docs/movement-iv-keeping-what-comes/01-the-drift-downward.md) and the first case in [How Revivals Were Lost](reader.html#docs/movement-iv-keeping-what-comes/02-how-revivals-were-lost.md).

**16. The harvest grows the thorns.** The same Wesley-voiced review traced the engine with terrible precision: true religion produces industry and frugality; these produce riches; "and as riches increase, so will pride, anger, and love of the world." Its conclusion: "The thorny ground is not an external hazard the good soil might avoid — it is what good soil becomes when the harvest is banked." Why it matters: prosperity is a fruit of revival and its most patient enemy; the soil diagnosis of [The Four Soils](reader.html#docs/movement-ii-what-is-your-soil/01-the-four-soils.md) must be repeated after the blessing, precisely when it feels least necessary. The full case is "The Harvest Grew the Thorns" in [How Revivals Were Lost](reader.html#docs/movement-iv-keeping-what-comes/02-how-revivals-were-lost.md).

**17. What survives is stored in hearts, not in structures.** The Baxter-voiced review testified that the county-wide association of ministers — the finest renewal structure of its age — was swept away by a single political winter, while what had been formed in the people of Kidderminster survived its pastor's removal, imprisonment, and approaching death: "it lived in persons, not in the association." Why it matters: the durable deposit of any move of God is formed people; every structure is scaffolding. This conviction shapes [Handing the Fire](reader.html#docs/movement-iv-keeping-what-comes/04-handing-the-fire.md) — where the handoff to the next generation happens at the family table, in warmth, person to person, not in the sanctuary.

**18. Tell me what stands at week eleven.** Three reviews — voiced from the eighteenth century, the sixth, and the era of the ancient catechumenate — independently found the same gap in a ten-week beginners' course: it ended at the exact moment of maximum need. The Wesley-voiced review: "Tell me what stands at week eleven, and I will tell you whether this system makes disciples or only makes beginnings." The Benedict-voiced review said it as a farmer would: "ground opened in a temporary community can be left standing open in the rain." Why it matters: awakening without formation is among the best-documented ways revivals die — see "Awakened, and Never Formed" in [How Revivals Were Lost](reader.html#docs/movement-iv-keeping-what-comes/02-how-revivals-were-lost.md). It is why Movement III insists on building the ongoing vessel — the bench of companions, the standing fellowship — *before* the rain.

## What stood

**19. Twelve panels, twelve confirmations.** Every one of the twelve panels was invited — in effect, dared — to break the conviction underneath this whole book: that the ordinary believer can hear God's voice in Scripture and prayer and obey it. None did. Twelve reviews, twelve confirmations, no qualifications — and the confirmation came most strongly from the reviewers best positioned to take it away. The review written in the voice of parables scholar Klyne Snodgrass ruled on the Sower itself: "A formation model whose core is hearing-and-obeying God has not imported a foreign concern into this text — it has found the text's own concern." Why it matters: everything this book asks of you rests on that conviction, and it was tested harder than anything else we own. It is the ground under [Hungry Hearts](reader.html#docs/movement-ii-what-is-your-soil/02-hungry-hearts.md) and every chapter after it.

**20. The core is not just sound — it is load-bearing, and it protects the sheep.** The final review ended with two verdicts that turned confirmation into strengthening. The measurement perspective ruled the hear-and-obey center *load-bearing*: every guardrail, every diagnostic, every caution in these materials presupposes it — take it away and there is nothing left to form. And the review voiced as Diane Langberg ruled it *protective*: "A believer taught that she herself can hear God in Scripture and prayer is harder to own than one taught that God's voice reaches her only through the man on the platform." Its last word on the subject deserves to be this chapter's last insight: "Sheep who know the Shepherd's voice are harder to steal." Teaching a congregation to hear God is not a threat to good order. It is the flock's best protection against every misuse this chapter has catalogued. That is the deepest reason [Hungry Hearts](reader.html#docs/movement-ii-what-is-your-soil/02-hungry-hearts.md) and [The Guardrails](reader.html#docs/movement-ii-what-is-your-soil/03-the-guardrails.md) belong to the same movement of this book.

## Reading these voices rightly

Let the framing be said once more, plainly, so that no reader carries away more than was claimed. Every insight above came from a structured internal review in which the reviews were *written in the voices* of trusted figures — shepherds, theologians, scientists, and clinicians — reconstructed from their published work. They were exercises in perspective, undertaken to stress-test this book's foundations before any church was asked to trust them. They are not endorsements, and no living or historical person reviewed or approved these materials. What the exercise yielded, we have tried to hand you honestly: the corrections were kept, the warnings were built into the book's commitments, and the debts are acknowledged — above all to the real men and women whose real published work made such perspective-taking possible at all. Test everything here the same way the foundations were tested: against Scripture, in prayer, and out loud with people you trust. *"But test everything; hold fast what is good"* (1 Thessalonians 5:21, ESV).
