# How Revivals Were Lost

> **Draft status:** working draft (DEV) — offered for prayer and honest discussion.

The previous chapter, [The Drift Downward](reader.html#docs/movement-iv-keeping-what-comes/01-the-drift-downward.md), named the law: spiritual life runs downhill the moment it stops receiving, at every scale, by default. This chapter opens the case file. Because the drift is not a theory — it has a history, and the history has names and dates. Wales, 1904. Northampton, 1735. Azusa Street, 1906. Cane Ridge, 1801. New York, 1957. Real fires fell in real churches, and in every case the record lets us watch what happened next — and in every case, sooner or later, something was lost.

Read that record honestly and a pattern emerges that ought to change how a church prays for revival. The fires were not lost in exotic ways. They were not stolen by persecution, mostly, or crushed by hostile governments, or argued away by clever skeptics. They were lost in a handful of recurring, recognizable, almost boring ways — the same few failure modes, century after century, like a farmer losing harvest after harvest to the same unfixed fence.

And here is this chapter's one sentence, the spine everything below hangs on: **none of these failure modes is exotic — every one of them is a preparation question.** Every mode in the record answers to something a church could have built, or asked, or settled *before* the rain fell. Which is why this book exists: not to teach you to summon the fire, which no one can, but to walk you through the preparation questions while there is still time to answer them.

So take them one at a time. Six ways revivals were lost. For each: what happened, why it happens, and where in this book the answer lives.

## The Form Outlived the Power

**What happened.** This is the oldest mode in the record and the most common. The revival comes; the revival gets a container — meetings, methods, buildings, schedules — as it must, for wine needs a skin. And then, over years, the life quietly departs while the container keeps running. The mission on Azusa Street, where the twentieth century's largest Christian movement was born in 1906, dwindled to a remnant and was gone by 1931 — while the movement it had birthed spread across the world. Welsh church membership peaked in 1905, in the very glow of the great revival, and declined continuously from that year forward — the structures full, then fuller, then hollowing. And behind these stand the two churches the risen Christ Himself diagnosed: Ephesus, every structure intact — works, toil, endurance, sound doctrine — and the first love abandoned; Sardis, with "the reputation of being alive," and dead (Revelation 3:1, ESV). Students of religious movements have watched this so consistently they gave it a name — the routinization of the extraordinary — and the record confirms it with almost mechanical regularity: the founding event fades; the forms remain; and the church's energy shifts, imperceptibly, from receiving the wine to polishing the skin.

**The mechanism, plainly.** Structure does not need fresh inflow to persist, and life does. So given enough time, every untended structure outlives the life it was built to hold — and a congregation can maintain "the appearance of godliness" long after "denying its power" (2 Timothy 3:5, ESV), usually without ever noticing the exchange. Nobody votes to become Sardis. Sardis is what a revival becomes by default when the receiving stops and the meetings continue.

**The preparation that answers it.** This one belongs to the chapter you have just read. [The Drift Downward](reader.html#docs/movement-iv-keeping-what-comes/01-the-drift-downward.md) gives the whole discipline: structures examined on a schedule rather than at a crisis; fruit measured instead of polish; ministries retired honorably when the wine has moved on; and the receiving — hearing God, obeying God — kept ruthlessly at the center of everything. A church that installs those habits *before* the rain has answered, in advance, the failure mode that has taken more revivals than any other.

## The Harvest Grew the Thorns

**What happened.** Jesus warned that among the soils there is one where the seed grows and then is strangled — "the cares of the world and the deceitfulness of riches and the desires for other things enter in and choke the word, and it proves unfruitful" (Mark 4:19, ESV). The record adds a twist the parable's first hearers might not have guessed: after a revival, the thorns often grow *from the harvest itself*. Nobody saw this more clearly than John Wesley, watching his own movement age. Near the end of his life he wrote down the mechanism with terrible precision: true religion produces industry and frugality, and these "cannot but produce riches. But as riches increase, so will pride, anger, and love of the world" — so that, he feared, "although the form of religion remains, the spirit is swiftly vanishing away." The revival makes people diligent; diligence makes them prosperous; prosperity makes them comfortable; and comfort quietly closes the valve that hunger had opened. The awakened church becomes the established church. The despised movement becomes respectable — and respectability has never once, in the whole record, been hungry.

**The mechanism, plainly.** Scripture named the accelerant long before Wesley did, and the drift chapter walked through it: it is not persecution that makes a people forget God; it is the full stomach (Deuteronomy 8:11–14). Hard times drive a church to its knees. Success invites it to stand back up, look around at what God has built, and begin — reasonably, gratefully, fatally — to protect it. The thorns do not attack the wheat. They just grow beside it, out of the same rich soil, until the wheat is shaded out.

**The preparation that answers it.** Wesley's own remedy was that those who gain all they can and save all they can must also *give* all they can — keeping the channel open so the blessing flows through rather than pooling. Deeper than any single remedy, though, is a church that has learned to keep watching its own soil after the harvest: the thorny ground is one of the four soils this book asked you to diagnose honestly in Movement II, and the diagnosis is not a one-time exam. It must be repeated in prosperity, precisely when it feels least necessary — and held safely under [The Guardrails](reader.html#docs/movement-ii-what-is-your-soil/03-the-guardrails.md), so that watching the soil never curdles into policing it. [The Drift Downward](reader.html#docs/movement-iv-keeping-what-comes/01-the-drift-downward.md) carries the standing discipline: prosperity is when the examination questions matter most, because comfort is drift's favorite accelerant and busyness its favorite camouflage.

## The Fire Housed in One Man

**What happened.** The Welsh revival of 1904–05 is remembered, rightly, alongside the name Evan Roberts — a young man God used mightily. And within about two years Roberts had collapsed under the weight of it and withdrawn from public ministry, and the revival that had been so identified with him had no second act; the region's chapels began the long decline already described. The record is tender here, and we should be too: Roberts was not a villain but a casualty — a man asked to carry, alone and continuously, what no one was built to carry. But the structural lesson stands, because the record also shows the contrast. Where fresh movements *endured* past their founding figure, students of those movements keep finding the same design: authority deliberately distributed into many ordinary hands rather than concentrated in one extraordinary pair. The Vineyard movement survived the death of its founder, John Wimber, in 1997, because succession had been planned before it was needed. The work outlived the man because the work had never been housed only in the man.

**The mechanism, plainly.** Paul saw this failure mode forming in Corinth within a generation of Pentecost and dismantled it in three sentences: "What then is Apollos? What is Paul? Servants through whom you believed... I planted, Apollos watered, but God gave the growth" (1 Corinthians 3:5–6, ESV). When a congregation quietly relocates its faith from the Giver to the instrument, two things follow. The instrument can no longer be told hard truth — who corrects the man God is so evidently using? — and the instrument cannot be succeeded, because the movement's actual object of trust is not transferable. Then the man burns out, or falls, or simply dies, and the fire that was housed in him goes with him. And a third thing, quieter: the man himself is crushed. A church that makes one leader carry the revival is not honoring him. It is consuming him.

**The preparation that answers it.** Two builds, both of them before the rain. First, a leadership culture in which hard truth can actually reach the person at the front — which is exactly the self-test [The Guardrails](reader.html#docs/movement-ii-what-is-your-soil/03-the-guardrails.md) requires every leadership to run on itself: *what happened to the last person who told us something we did not want to hear?* If the honest answer is "nothing good," your church is already building this failure mode, fire or no fire. Second, the deliberate distribution of the work: Movement III's whole argument is that a church prepares by forming a *bench* — many ordinary members made into companions and group leaders — so that whatever God sends is received by a body, never balanced on a single neck. A revival housed in fifty formed hearts has fifty times the redundancy of a revival housed in one gifted man, and no one of them has to be crushed by it.

## Awakened, and Never Formed

**What happened.** This is the mode the numbers document best, and the numbers are sobering. At Cane Ridge, Kentucky, in 1801, somewhere between ten and twenty thousand people gathered — perhaps a tenth of the whole state's population — in scenes of extraordinary spiritual power. Charles Finney's campaigns stirred so many revivals across upstate New York that the region earned a nickname we will come back to. And yet Finney himself, late in life, looked back with an honesty that should be required reading for every revival-hungry church: the later revivals, he admitted, were of much shorter duration, and "fewer of the converts make stable and efficient Christians." He had begun to ponder, in his own words' spirit if not their letter, the problem of his converts who became backsliders. The awakening had been real. The forming had not happened.

The twentieth century then ran the experiment with counters attached, and the results deserve a church's full attention. In the 1957 New York crusade, roughly 57,000 people recorded decisions for Christ; the churches of the city gained somewhere between six and ten thousand new members. In one carefully studied 1976 crusade in the Pacific Northwest, about fifteen percent of inquirers were ever incorporated into a congregation — and of those who were, eight out of ten *already had a friend or relative in that church*. But the finding inside the finding is the one to underline: retention tracked the follow-up structure, not the size or heat of the meeting. Where churches followed up their referrals aggressively — a visit, a companion, a place to belong within days — the great majority stayed. Where they did not, the decisions evaporated. The Graham organization itself understood this and built famously thorough machinery — counselors trained for weeks in advance, every decision card in a local pastor's hands within a day — and it was precisely where that machinery connected to a ready congregation that the fruit remained. The harvest was gathered where there were barns.

**The mechanism, plainly.** Jesus described it in the parable this book is built on: the seed on rocky ground springs up *immediately, with joy* — and withers, "since it had no root" (Matthew 13:5–6, 20–21, ESV). An awakening produces beginnings; only formation produces roots; and formation requires what a stirred crowd does not automatically contain — a person alongside, a small community, a path, a practice. Thousands stirred and no companions to walk with them is not a revival kept. It is seed on rock, at scale. He "appointed you that you should go and bear fruit and that your fruit should abide" (John 15:16, ESV) — and abiding fruit, in the whole record, grows on vines somebody trellised in advance.

**The preparation that answers it.** This failure mode is the reason Movement III gave you its second command: *build the vessel before the rain.* The bench of formed small-group leaders, the companions trained ahead of need, the pathway a newly awakened heart can actually be walked down — all of it exists so that if God sends three hundred souls in half a year, as He did in Edwards's Northampton, there is somewhere for them to land on day two. The record could not be clearer: the churches that kept their converts were not the ones with the biggest meetings. They were the ones with the readiest companions.

## The Fire Turned on Itself

**What happened.** Revivals generate controversy the way fire generates smoke — the record shows scarcely one without it. At Cane Ridge, the strange physical phenomena — people falling, crying out, seized by the jerking movements observers struggled to explain — set Christians arguing about the revival almost before it cooled. Finney's "new measures" drew the charge, from within the church, that his revivals were manufactured rather than sent. And such arguments do not stay abstract: they consume exactly the energy, attention, and mutual trust that the fresh work was running on. A church that spends its revival year litigating the revival has found a use for the fire other than being warmed by it.

But the record is honest in both directions, and so must we be. Not every controversy kills a work; some controversies *prune* it. The Vineyard movement drew hard boundaries more than once — including, in 1995, separating from a famous renewal center whose excesses it would not own — and those painful decisions strengthened the movement rather than weakening it. Discernment is not division. The failure mode is not that a church asks hard questions of what is happening in its midst — it must. The failure mode is the *manner*: quarrels instead of discernment, factions instead of judgment, the revival made a party question with winners and losers.

**The mechanism, plainly.** Paul, watching factions form around personalities and manifestations in Corinth, and Galatian churches tearing at each other, put the physics of it in one sentence: "But if you bite and devour one another, watch out that you are not consumed by one another" (Galatians 5:15, ESV). Consumed — the fire's own verb. A congregation has one supply of attention and trust, and every hour of faction is drawn from the same account as every hour of prayer. Division does not merely distract from the fire. It is fed *by* the fire, burning the revival as fuel.

**The preparation that answers it.** A church cannot learn to disagree well in the middle of an outpouring, any more than a crew can learn seamanship in the storm. That is why Movement III begins where it begins: with a leadership that has *already* become a fellowship of the heart — practiced, before the rain, at hearing God together, telling each other the truth in love, and holding differences without splitting. And it is why [The Guardrails](reader.html#docs/movement-ii-what-is-your-soil/03-the-guardrails.md) matter here too, for a community whose members' interior lives are held *for* them rather than over them is a community where discernment does not curdle into accusation. Churches that settled how they would handle disagreement before God sent them something worth disagreeing about kept both the fire and each other.

## The Dry Season Read as Failure

**What happened.** Every founding event in the record faded. Every one. Northampton's extraordinary half-year gave way to ordinary years; the great encampments dispersed; even the recent outpouring at Asbury in 2023 was deliberately drawn to a close by the school's own leadership after sixteen days — a stewardship decision, not a defeat. The fading of the *peak* is not a failure mode; it is how outpourings work, and the previous chapters have already said why. The failure mode is what churches *did* with the fading. Some read it as God's departure and quit — the prayer meetings that had preceded the fire dissolving quietly after it, the very disciplines that had prepared the ground abandoned as though they had only ever been scaffolding for the exciting part. Others read it as a problem to be fixed and set about manufacturing the feeling again — whipping the same ground harder and harder for diminishing returns, until the region Finney's campaigns had swept again and again earned its grim nickname: the burned-over district, a countryside so exhausted by wave upon wave of revival that observers judged there was nothing left in it to ignite. Quitting and forcing look like opposites. They are the same misreading: both assume that when the felt intensity subsides, God has finished — or failed.

**The mechanism, plainly.** This book has already shown you the instrument for this one, and it fits in a sentence: dryness is at least four different things wearing the same gray coat, and one of them is God's own deepest work. A church that has never learned the difference will diagnose every dry season as disconnection — and then either despair of it or try to drown it in manufactured noise, when Scripture's counsel for the faithful in the dark is neither: "let him who walks in darkness and has no light trust in the name of the LORD and rely on his God" (Isaiah 50:10, ESV). The season after the rain is when roots go down — it is where all the keeping actually happens — and it is precisely the season the excitement-trained church has no category for. "Let us not grow weary of doing good, for in due season we will reap, if we do not give up" (Galatians 6:9, ESV). The revivals lost to this mode were not lost in the dark. They were lost when the church, meeting the dark, gave up — or lit its own torches.

**The preparation that answers it.** [When the Ground Goes Dry](reader.html#docs/movement-iii-preparing-the-soil/04-when-the-ground-goes-dry.md) exists for exactly this moment, and its differential — the four kinds of dryness, sorted by the one question of where the desire points — should be in the working knowledge of every leader before your church prays its first revival prayer. A church that can tell drift from stripping will neither comfort what needs waking nor crush what God is deepening. It will do the one thing the excitement-trained church cannot: keep showing up, trusting, in the year the feelings went quiet — which, in the record, is the year that decided everything.

## One More, for the Long Game

There is a seventh way revivals were lost, and it is the slowest and perhaps the most heartbreaking: the fire kept faithfully for a whole generation, and never successfully handed to the next one — children raised inside the revival who inherited the buildings, the hymns, and the vocabulary, but not the thing itself. New England ran that tragedy on a thirty-year clock, and the research on how faith does and does not cross generations is among the most useful material this book has to offer. It is also its own chapter's territory: [Handing the Fire](reader.html#docs/movement-iv-keeping-what-comes/04-handing-the-fire.md) takes the whole question — what cannot be handed down, what can, warmth as the carrier, and the household practices that provoke a child to ask. Read it as this catalog's final entry, given the room it deserves.

## What the Keepers Had in Common

Now step back from the six modes and notice what they share.

Not one of them required an enemy. Form outliving power, thorns growing from harvest, fire housed in one man, converts left unformed, quarrels eating the flame, dry seasons misread — every one is a failure of *preparation*, not a defeat by opposition. Which means every one of them can be addressed by an ordinary church, in ordinary time, before anything extraordinary happens. That is the strange comfort of this sober chapter: the record's bad news is answerable news.

And the record shows the answers working. The praying communities whose intercession preceded the Hebrides awakening had been at it long before the fire fell — the fire found a wick already trimmed. The Moravians at Herrnhut turned their moment of visitation into a watch of continuous prayer that they sustained, in relays, for a hundred years — a structure built not to replace the fire but to keep receiving it. The crusade converts who were still walking with Christ a year later were, overwhelmingly, the ones met by churches whose follow-up was built and staffed before the stadium ever filled. Movements that outlived their founders had settled succession while the founder still stood. In every case the pattern is the same, and it is this movement's whole message: **the churches that kept what came were the ones that had built for keeping before the rain fell.**

That is not a technique for obligating God. Nothing obligates God. It is simply the difference between the wise and foolish builders, applied to the most precious thing a church can ever receive. The rain, when it comes, falls on prepared and unprepared alike; the record of what happened next was written, every time, by the preparation. Break up your fallow ground — and while you are at it, mend the barn, train the companions, learn the dry seasons, and teach your leaders to hear the truth. It is the time to seek the LORD. It is also, therefore, the time to build.
