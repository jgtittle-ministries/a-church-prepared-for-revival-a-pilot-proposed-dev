# The Drift Downward

> **Draft status:** working draft (DEV) — offered for prayer and honest discussion.

The first page of this book named its two convictions in a single breath: *a desire for more, and a recognition of the drift downward.* Every movement so far has been unfolding the desire. This chapter unfolds the drift — because a church that wants revival without understanding the drift is asking for a gift it is not yet prepared to keep.

Start with three scenes you have probably witnessed.

A believer — earnest, once on fire — notices one day that her prayer has gone quiet. Not stopped, exactly. Thinned. She cannot name the month it happened.

A family — church every Sunday for three generations — discovers that the grandchildren keep the building and the vocabulary but not the thing itself. Nobody rebelled. It just faded.

A congregation — busy, solvent, respected — has more meetings than it had twenty years ago and less fire. The old members can name the year the temperature changed. Nobody else noticed anything change at all.

Three problems, we assume. Three committees, probably. A discipleship problem, a family-ministry problem, a church-renewal problem — each with its own shelf of books.

This chapter makes a different claim, and it is the claim on which the whole of Movement IV rests: **these are not three problems. They are one law, showing up at three scales.** A soul cools, a household cools, a congregation cools — by the same dynamic, for the same reason, with the same remedy. The law can be stated in one sentence:

**Spiritual life runs downhill the moment it stops receiving — because created life was never self-sustaining. Only the Source is.**

If that is true, it changes how a church prepares for revival, how it reads its own decline, and above all what it must build to keep whatever God sends. So let us take it slowly.

## Ruin Without a Vandal

The New Testament has a verse that pictures the whole thing. "Therefore we must pay much closer attention to what we have heard, lest we drift away from it" (Hebrews 2:1, ESV throughout). The word translated *drift* appears only here in the New Testament, and it paints a boat slipping its mooring — no storm, no leak, no hand on the rope. Just the current, and time. The drifting is passive, silent, and imperceptible while it is happening. And notice the direction built into the picture: no one drifts *toward* the harbor.

The Old Testament paints the same law on land. The wise man walks past the field of the sluggard and finds thorns, nettles, and the stone wall broken down (Proverbs 24:30–34) — and here is the detail worth sitting with: nothing attacked that field. There is no destructive act anywhere in the account. Time plus inattention was sufficient. Ecclesiastes states it as a plain proverb of maintenance: "Through sloth the roof sinks in, and through indolence the house leaks" (Ecclesiastes 10:18). Ruin by commission needs a vandal. Ruin by omission needs only a calendar.

That is the disturbing part of the drift, and also the diagnostic part: **nobody has to do anything for it to happen.** The believer whose prayer thinned did not decide anything. The family did not vote to lose the faith. The congregation never passed a motion to cool. Drift is what spiritual life does *by default* when the receiving stops — which is why it is always noticed late, and why Scripture's counsel against it comes with a dosage: "exhort one another every day, as long as it is called 'today,' that none of you may be hardened by the deceitfulness of sin" (Hebrews 3:13). Every day — because the current runs every day.

Scripture even names the drift's favorite accelerant, and it is not persecution. It is prosperity. "Take care lest you forget the LORD your God... lest, when you have eaten and are full... then your heart be lifted up, and you forget the LORD your God" (Deuteronomy 8:11–14). Hard times drive a people to their knees; the full stomach quietly closes the valve. Notice, all through Deuteronomy, that *remember* is always a command and *forget* is always the passive danger — there is no verse that says "take care lest you remember." Memory needs infrastructure — words on doorposts, stones in rivers, feasts on calendars — because memory, left alone, decays. Forgetting needs nothing at all.

## Why the Slope Only Runs One Way

But *why* should spiritual life run downhill? Why is there no drift toward the harbor?

You may know that the physical world has this character too — everything left untended cools, scatters, and runs down; a hot cup goes cold in a cold room, never the reverse. We borrow that only as a picture, and we say so plainly: it is an illustration of the truth, not a proof of it. Scripture carries the claim. The picture just helps us see it.

Here is the claim, and it is older than any physics. **Created things were never self-sustaining.** We did not make ourselves, and we do not keep ourselves. In the fourth century, Athanasius wrote that created nature "is of a fleeting sort, and weak and mortal, if composed of itself only" — everything that was called out of nothing begins sliding back toward nothing the moment it is cut off from the One who called it. Aquinas later gave the same doctrine an exact picture: being is to creatures as light is to air. Air does not store light. The room is bright only while the source shines. This is not a fringe idea; it is a standing doctrine of the church — God did not wind creation up and walk away; He upholds it, moment by moment, or it does not persist at all.

Now watch what that means for the drift. The downhill slope is not the Fall's invention, and it is not a punishment. It is simply what creatureliness is: dependent life, alive by receiving. What sin did was not create the slope — sin cut the supply line. Cut off from the Source, a creature does the only thing an unsupplied creature can do: it runs down.

And here is the finding that should reframe everything a church believes about maintenance: **keeping came before the Fall.** "The LORD God took the man and put him in the garden of Eden to work it and keep it" (Genesis 2:15). Even unfallen Eden required tending. And the Hebrew word pair there — *work it and keep it* — is the same vocabulary used elsewhere for the priests' duty in the tabernacle. Tending the garden was liturgy. Maintenance is not the curse's chore; **maintenance is worship**, and it was Adam's calling before it was anyone's burden. The same verb keeps the soul: "Only take care, and keep your soul diligently, lest you forget the things that your eyes have seen" (Deuteronomy 4:9).

Test the claim against Scripture's own pictures of spiritual health, and something remarkable turns up: **every one of them is a picture of continuous supply.** The tree planted by streams of water. The vine and the branches — "apart from me you can do nothing" (John 15:5), and the severed branch does not remain a branch; it withers by default. The body "nourished and knit together" as it is continuously supplied through its Head (Colossians 2:19). Mercies that are "new every morning" (Lamentations 3:22–23) — replenishment quoted at the morning rate. Rivers of living water flowing *out* of the believer — a conduit, never a reservoir. Search as long as you like: **Scripture has no image of stored, self-maintaining spiritual life.** Not one. There is no cistern verse, no verse where yesterday's filling covers tomorrow. Paul compresses the whole doctrine into one sentence with two present tenses: "Though our outer self is wasting away, our inner self is being renewed day by day" (2 Corinthians 4:16). Wasting and renewal, both ongoing, both *now* — the outer running down, the inner running only on inflow.

So the soul is not a stone, which keeps its shape by being inert. The soul is more like a flame — ordered, bright, and alive precisely *because* something flows through it, and dissolving the moment the flow stops. "Abide in me" is not a devotional flourish. It is the supply-condition of a created life, stated as a command.

## Drift Is Not the Dark Night

Before this law goes one step further, it must be fenced — because misapplied, it will wound exactly the person it most needs to help.

If untended souls run down, then a believer whose prayer has gone flat should read her own dryness as drift and be alarmed — yes? **No.** Not necessarily, and getting this wrong is, as this book said in [When the Ground Goes Dry](../movement-iii-preparing-the-soil/04-when-the-ground-goes-dry.md), the one unforgivable pastoral mistake in a preparing church. Dryness is at least four different things wearing the same gray coat, and one of them — the stripping the old writers called the dark night — is not drift at all but God's own deep work, His presence pressing in beyond the soul's capacity to feel it. That chapter sorts the four with care, and every leader in a preparing church should have its little table nearly memorized.

Here, one sentence carries the whole distinction: **the drifting soul does not ache.** The believer in God's stripping wants Him and cannot feel Him — desire intact and starving, the absence hurting, the feet still showing up. The drifting believer has stopped wanting and hasn't noticed — the distance doesn't particularly hurt, the world still tastes fine, and the practice lapsed without grief. Same flat prayers on the outside; opposite conditions underneath; opposite remedies. The dry-but-aching soul is told to *wait* and be accompanied. The drifting soul is told, kindly, to *wake and turn*.

So mark the three features of drift, because they are this chapter's working definition:

**Drift is always downhill.** Nobody drifts into deeper prayer by neglect. The current has one direction.

**Drift is never a punishment.** This must be said pastorally and clearly: the cooling is not God pushing you away, and it is not His verdict on you. He has not moved. "He does not faint or grow weary" (Isaiah 40:28) — the supply never tired of supplying. Drift is simply what a receiving life does when it stops receiving — a law, like gravity, not a sentence, like a judge's.

**Drift is always recoverable — by returning to the Source.** Not by trying harder in place, not by polishing the routines, but by reconnecting the supply line: "Draw near to God, and he will draw near to you" (James 4:8). At any point. From any distance. That is where this chapter will end, because it is where the whole matter ends.

## A Soul Cools, a House Cools, a Church Cools

Now walk the one law through its three scales, and watch it wear three different sets of clothes.

**The soul.** At the scale of one heart, drift looks like the slow multiplication of competing loves. The Old Testament measured kings by a wholeness-test — this king's heart "was wholly true," that one's was not — and the Hebrew word for that wholeness is from the same root as *shalom*. A heart gathered on God is a heart at peace; a heart scattered across many little loves is restless, expensive to run, and cooling — often while busier than ever, for busyness is not the opposite of drift but its favorite camouflage. And heart-gathering is itself something received, not achieved; the psalmist asks it *of God*: "unite my heart to fear your name" (Psalm 86:11). The believer's counter-drift practices — daily Scripture, honest prayer, the mutual exhortation of Hebrews 3:13 — are not merit-making. They are the supply line, kept open on purpose, at the frequency the drift actually runs: daily.

**The household.** At the scale of a family, the law runs across time, and its signature is the fade between generations — fire in the grandparents, forms in the parents, a photograph on the mantel for the children. This book gives that scale its own full chapter, [Handing the Fire](04-handing-the-fire.md), and here we need only the law underneath it: faith is not heritable, so every generation starts at zero — but the household's *idols* transmit by themselves, free of charge, absorbed through the pores. Downhill is free; uphill takes deliberate work in every generation. That is why the Shema soaks the whole day — sit, walk, lie down, rise — rather than booking an hour a week. An hour a week cannot carry water uphill against a culture flowing down.

**The congregation.** At the scale of a church, we are not left to build the diagnosis ourselves, because the risen Christ has already written it — seven times, by name, to real congregations, in letters this book examined in [The Seven Letters](../movement-i-do-you-want-a-revival/02-the-seven-letters.md). Read those letters again with this chapter's law in hand, and they light up like a lampstand. Ephesus is the cooling curve exactly: works, toil, endurance, sound doctrine — every structure intact — "but I have this against you, that you have abandoned the love you had at first" (Revelation 2:4). Form outlasting fire. Sardis is death by nothing in particular: "You have the reputation of being alive, but you are dead" (Revelation 3:1) — and the letter names no persecution, no heresy, no catastrophe at Sardis. Nothing attacked that field either. The roof sank in through sloth, and the prescription Christ writes is the maintenance imperative in His own voice: "strengthen what remains and is about to die" (Revelation 3:2). And Laodicea — "I need nothing" — is the drift signature at full corporate scale: the church that stopped aching and didn't notice, comfortable at the precise temperature of the room around it. A cooling church does not usually feel cold. It feels *fine*. That is the diagnosis.

One law. Three scales. The believer's thinned prayer, the grandchild's inherited photograph, and Sardis's reputation are the same event at different sizes: a receiving life that stopped receiving, cooling on schedule.

## The Wineskin

There is one more form the drift takes, and it is the one revival-seeking churches most need to study, because it is the one that eats revivals.

"And no one puts new wine into old wineskins. If he does, the new wine will burst the skins and it will be spilled, and the skins will be destroyed. But new wine must be put into fresh wineskins" (Luke 5:37–38).

We usually read that as a word about the Pharisees, safely aimed at somebody else. Read it instead as what it also is: **a law of institutions**, stated by the Lord who knew exactly what happens to fresh works of God. New wine is still fermenting — alive, expanding, exerting pressure. A new skin stretches with it. But skins do not stay new. They dry, stiffen, and set in the shape of the wine they once held — and then the next new wine bursts them.

Here is the pattern, and the record of revivals confirms it with almost mechanical regularity. Every fresh work of God gets a container, and *should*: wine needs a skin, fire needs a hearth. Structure is not the enemy — the prayer meeting gets a schedule, the converts get a class, the movement gets a handbook. In the first generation the structure serves the life, and serves it well. But structure, unlike life, does not require fresh inflow to persist. So the structure outlives the life it was built to hold — and then, imperceptibly, the church's energy shifts from receiving the wine to polishing the skin. The meeting continues after the praying has thinned. The form is defended more fiercely than the fire it was formed around. Students of religious movements have watched this so often they gave it a name — the routinization of charisma: the extraordinary settling into routine: what began as fire becomes, within a generation or two, a fireplace, and then a photograph of a fireplace. [Handing the Fire](04-handing-the-fire.md) tells the sobering New England version of the story — an ocean crossed for living faith, and one generation later a synod inventing halfway membership for the children of the fire.

But now the honest complication, because the wineskin is not simply a villain. The record shows the opposite failure too: revivals that built no structure at all spilled their wine on the ground. Where fresh works endured, they endured because somebody built vessels — organized follow-up, small communities, disciplined prayer rhythms — and in the best-measured modern cases, whether a new convert was still walking with a church a year later tracked the follow-up structure far more closely than the size or heat of the original meeting. The founding event almost always fades; what carries the work forward is what was built to hold it. So the wineskin both *keeps* the wine and *cools* it — the same process, two faces. A church that refuses structure loses the revival in a year. A church that enshrines structure keeps a beautifully maintained skin, empty.

This is why the drift-law needed its earlier caution, and here it earns its keep: **not all order is life.** A stone is extremely stable; nothing about it is alive. Scripture's image for the over-ordered heart is exactly this — the heart of stone, which God's remedy is not to preserve but to replace with a heart of flesh. An old wineskin is order of the stone kind: rigid, defended, stable the way dead things are stable. The question a church must learn to ask of every structure it owns is never merely "is this orderly?" A crystal is orderly. The question is whether the order is *alive* — ordered by the flow still moving through it, or ordered by the shape of a flow that stopped years ago.

## Keeping the Wineskin Supple

So what does a church actually *do*? Jesus' word implies the answer: new wine needs fresh skins — and a church that expects God to keep pouring must keep its skins supple. Suppleness does not happen by default; default is hardening. It must be designed. Practically, designed renewal looks like this:

**Expect new wine.** Build every structure with the stated assumption that God will one day do something it did not anticipate. A structure founded with its own future revision written into it — "this exists to hold this life; when the life moves, this will move" — ages differently from a structure founded as a monument.

**Examine on a schedule, not at a crisis.** Once a year, ask of each ministry, program, and standing meeting two questions, out loud, with the answers written down: *What life is this currently holding?* and *Would we build this today?* Not "is it running well" — a program can be polished to a high shine and hold nothing. The examination is the maintenance discipline of institutions, and like all maintenance it looks expensive precisely because the cost of skipping it stays invisible until the breakdown, when it comes due all at once.

**Measure fruit, not polish.** Attendance tells you a structure is functioning. It does not tell you the structure is alive. Ask instead the questions this book's earlier movements have been building toward: is anyone hearing God here? obeying at cost? being formed? If a structure's honest answer has been "no" for years, the structure is a skin without wine, however smooth its operation.

**Retire structures honorably.** A church must be able to end things *with thanksgiving* — naming what God did through a ministry in its season, and laying it down without treating the ending as failure or betrayal. If nothing in your church has been retired in twenty years, your wineskins are not supple; they are simply unexamined. And let this book bind itself to its own rule: everything this pilot proposes — its groups, its rhythms, its instruments — is a wineskin, offered for the wine's sake, and must remain examinable, refreshable, and if need be, retirable, by the same discipline. The moment any structure here becomes too sacred to question, it has become exactly what this chapter warns against.

**Keep the receiving at the center.** The final test of every structure is whether it still does the only thing spiritual structure is for: holding people near the Source. A meeting that keeps people praying is a supply line. A meeting people maintain *instead of* praying is a skin being polished. The instant maintaining the vessel competes with receiving the wine, the hardening has begun.

None of this is bureaucratic advice. It is the wineskin's version of "abide" — the congregation-scale form of keeping the supply line open. Souls drift daily, so souls need daily practices. Structures harden over years, so structures need scheduled renewal. Same law, matched cadence.

## The Other Law: Return

Now let the chapter say plainly why it was worth naming so sobering a law.

You do not study gravity to despair of flying; you study it to fly. A law that is named can be worked with — and this law, read to its end, is not a sentence but a supply schedule. Read it again, all the way to its clause: spiritual life runs downhill *the moment it stops receiving.* The clause is not fine print. The clause is the gospel in this chapter. **Nothing in the law dooms a church to cool — the law only describes what happens apart from the Source, and no soul, household, or congregation has to be apart.** A church that keeps receiving does not cool. The flame that is fed does not go out — however ordinary the years, however long between rains.

And the drift's remedy is not heroic. It is directional. Because drift was never a punishment, return is never a groveling; because the Source never moved, the way back is exactly as long as the turning. This is why the risen Christ's prescription to the coolest orthodox church in Asia is so startlingly practical — "Remember therefore from where you have fallen; repent, and do the works you did at first" (Revelation 2:5): measure the distance honestly, call the cooling what it is, and reconnect through the very practices the love first flowed through. It is why the harshest letter of the seven ends not with a verdict but with a knock at the door, an offer of supper, and a seat on a throne. And it is why the drift's accounting can never out-run the mercy's: the drift compounds daily, but "his mercies... are new every morning" (Lamentations 3:22–23). The replenishment is quoted at the same rate as the loss.

Even what the drift steals is recoverable, and Scripture names the treasure precisely. The whole heart — the gathered, at-rest, *shalom*-rooted heart — is not a lost golden age; it is the standing offer of the Source to anyone who turns back, at any age, from any distance: "unite my heart to fear your name." The saints did not finish as people who never drifted. They finished as people who kept returning, and who built their lives — and, when they were wise, their churches — around the returning: daily bread asked for daily, mercies collected at the morning rate, exhortation exchanged while it is still called *today*.

So this movement of the book begins where the whole book has been heading. Do you want a revival? Then understand what you are asking for: an outpouring — the great inrush of the very supply this chapter has been describing — poured into vessels that will begin, immediately and by nature, to leak and to harden. That is not a reason to withhold the prayer. It is the reason to build as you pray: souls kept daily, households pumping uphill on purpose, structures examined while they are still supple, and everything — everything — arranged around the one non-negotiable, which is receiving. The Bible does not end with a monument, a museum, or a full reservoir. It ends with "the river of the water of life, bright as crystal, flowing from the throne of God and of the Lamb" (Revelation 22:1) — flow, present tense, forever. The city at the end of the story does not cool, for one reason only: it is never, for a moment, apart from its Source.

Until then, the word over the garden is still the word over the church: work it, and keep it. "They who wait for the LORD shall renew their strength" (Isaiah 40:31) — His strength for our weariness, exchanged at the point of return, every time. The drift downward is real, and it is a law. But so is that.
