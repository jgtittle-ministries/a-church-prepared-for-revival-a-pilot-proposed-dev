# Deciding Together — A Path for the Lead Pastor and Board

> **Draft status:** working draft (DEV) — offered for prayer and honest discussion.

This page is for the room where the decision will actually be made — the lead pastor and the board, elders, or council who carry responsibility for the congregation's direction. [Start Here](start-here.md) told you how to read this book. This page is about the harder thing: how to *decide* — together, honestly, at a pace worthy of the question — whether your church should step into the pilot this book proposes. It ends with a path either to an honest yes or to an honorable no, and it treats both as faithful outcomes.

## What kind of decision this is

Be clear about the category first, because boards decide well or badly depending on what they think they are deciding. This is not a program adoption. A program is something a church *adds* — a curriculum purchased, a staffer assigned, a start date announced — and if it disappoints, it is quietly retired and little was risked. What this book proposes is **culture change**: a change in how the leadership itself hears God, how the congregation tells the truth about its soul, and what the church counts as fruit. And culture change obeys different laws than programs, laws that anyone who has led real organizational change will recognize:

- **It runs on leadership energy, and leadership energy is the scarce resource.** Not endorsement — *energy*: hours on the lead pastor's actual calendar, attention in the board's actual meetings, across seasons, not weeks. A church whose leadership is already stretched by a building program, a staff transition, or an unresolved conflict does not lack the desire for this; it lacks the fuel, for now — and it is better to know that in the deciding room than in the third season.
- **The curve dips before it climbs.** Every real change effort has a middle season where the old momentum has been disturbed and the new fruit is not yet visible — where things feel slower and harder than they did before anything started. Boards that expected the dip walk through it; boards that expected a brochure curve conclude the effort failed and pull the plug at exactly the wrong moment. The record of revivals in Movement IV and the record of organizational change agree completely on this point.
- **It spreads person-to-person, through credible lives — not by announcement.** Nothing here can be launched from a platform. It moves the way the Gospel itself moved through households: someone whose life visibly changed, trusted by the next person, at the speed of relationship. Which is why the second question below is not optional.

Jesus put the cost-counting *before* the commitment, and He put it there as a command, not a concession: *"For which of you, desiring to build a tower, does not first sit down and count the cost, whether he has enough to complete it?"* — and again, in the same breath, the king who *"sit[s] down first and deliberate[s] whether he is able with ten thousand to meet him who comes against him with twenty thousand"* (Luke 14:28, 31, ESV). Both parables are energy audits. Sitting down first to count is not a lack of faith. It is the obedience the passage actually asks for.

## The path — six meetings

The four movements are short enough to read one per meeting. The path is six meetings, unhurried — two weeks to a month apart, and no fewer than six weeks from first to last, because a decision of this kind needs calendar time for God to speak into — and each of the first four ends with one written question the board answers before it moves on. Answered in writing matters: it keeps the deciding honest, and it gives a later season something to return to.

1. **Movement I — Do You Want a Revival?** The gate question: *do we want this, at the stated cost — or do we want the results without the preparation?* Both answers are common; only one of them should proceed.
2. **Movement II — What Is Your Soil?** Before imagining any congregational diagnostic, the board turns the movement's questions on *itself*: each member answers the soil questions personally, and the board runs [the Guardrails](movement-ii-what-is-your-soil/03-the-guardrails.md)' bad-news test on its own last twelve months — *what happened to the last person who told us something we did not want to hear?* The gate question: *are we willing to be diagnosed before we diagnose anyone?*
3. **Movement III — Preparing the Soil.** This is the meeting where the hardest sentences in the book arrive, and a board should meet them in the reading, not discover them after a yes: the leadership is formed *first*, and the senior-most leader places himself under a [named covering outside the congregation](movement-iii-preparing-the-soil/05-who-watches-the-shepherds.md), publicly. The gate question: *is each of us — starting at the top — willing to be plowed first?*
4. **Movement IV — Keeping What Comes.** The gate question: *can we name, specifically, what we would do differently from the churches that lost it — and where our own wineskin is already stiff?*
5. **Discernment.** Not a discussion meeting — a listening one. The book's whole premise is that God still speaks and means to be obeyed; let the decision be made the way the pilot would live. Scripture open, unhurried silence, each member listening and then telling what they heard — not *what do we think* but *what do we hear?* If the board has never prayed this way together, this meeting is itself a foretaste of what a yes would mean, and that is exactly the information a deciding board needs.
6. **Decision.** Yes, no, or *not now* — spoken plainly, recorded honestly, owned together.

## The three questions at the center

Everything above funnels into three questions. If the board can answer all three honestly, the decision will nearly make itself.

**1. The energy question.** *Do we have the leadership energy for culture change, in this season — and what will we stop doing to make room for it?* That last clause is the test of seriousness. Energy is conserved; a leadership calendar that adds this without subtracting something has already decided how the story ends. If the honest answer is "not in this season," say so and keep the date on which you will ask again — a *not now* from a clear-eyed board is worth more than a yes that quietly starves.

**2. The plow-us-first question.** *Will the leadership circle walk it before anyone else does?* A yes to this pilot means the lead pastor and board become the first [Getting Started](https://jgtittle-ministries.github.io/fellowship-of-the-heart-pilot-at-cca/reader.html#docs/getting-started/index.md) cohort themselves — actual weeks, actual practices, actual stories told to one another — before a single congregational group opens. Not because it is symbolically fitting, but because it is the mechanism: *"A disciple is not above his teacher, but everyone when he is fully trained will be like his teacher"* (Luke 6:40, ESV) — a congregation cannot be led where its leadership has not walked, and it will always know whether the platform has been plowed. And notice the mercy built into this order: the leadership cohort is also the pilot's real first test, run at the smallest and most private scale. If the leadership circle cannot sustain its own cohort — cannot keep the rhythm, cannot tell each other the truth — then the decision has honestly made itself, quietly, at almost no cost to the congregation. That is not failure. That is the cheapest, kindest place the answer could possibly have been found.

**3. The weep question.** *Are we ready to know what our diagnostics would tell us?* The standard this whole body of work holds is one sentence: **measure nothing you will not weep over.** So ask it before the first congregational question is ever asked: if an honest reading of our soil came back hard — hungrier than Sunday is feeding, dryer than the platform believes — would it land on this board as a grief that drives us to prayer, or as a performance problem to be managed? A board that would receive it as management is not wicked; it is simply not ready to hold a measure, and the diagnostics should wait until its heart is in the room. The Lord Himself read a city's condition and delivered the verdict *weeping* (Luke 19:41–44); that is the posture that qualifies a leadership to look at soil. Practically, this question has a written form: [the Guardrails](movement-ii-what-is-your-soil/03-the-guardrails.md) adopted by the board, and the signable [Measurement Covenant](https://jgtittle-ministries.github.io/fellowship-of-the-heart-pilot-at-cca/reader.html#docs/shared/measurement-covenant.md) signed by every leader who will ever see a result — both *before* the first instrument is used.

## What a yes is — and what it is not

A yes is not "we like the book." Written down, a yes is these five commitments, in this order:

1. The leadership circle forms and walks **Getting Started** itself, as the first cohort, to completion.
2. The four written adoptions of Season One, signed before any congregational group opens: **the Guardrails**, **[Who Watches the Shepherds](movement-iii-preparing-the-soil/05-who-watches-the-shepherds.md)** (including the member's channel), **the Measurement Covenant**, and **the civil-authority line** with your state's actual reporting requirements on the page.
3. A **named covering outside the congregation** for the senior-most leader, publicly known.
4. A **season-by-season pace** — the pilot proceeds one season at a time ([The Pilot, Season by Season](movement-iii-preparing-the-soil/06-the-pilot-season-by-season.md)), with a standing, written permission to pause or stop at any season boundary. Stopping honestly is governance working, not the vision failing.
5. The board keeps reading — this book once a year while the pilot runs, and the bad-news test annually, on itself.

And a yes is *not*: a guarantee of revival (no such guarantee exists, and this book says so on its first pages); a purchase; a staffing decision; or an irreversible act. Every season boundary is a door.

## The honorable no

Some boards will read all four movements, pray meeting five honestly, and conclude: *not us, or not now.* Hear this from the book itself: **that outcome is not a failure of this process — it is the process working.** The Lord who told the tower parable did not tell it to shame the man who counted and found he could not build; He told it to spare him the half-built tower. A board that counts the cost and declines has done exactly what was asked, and it has protected its congregation from the far worse outcome — a pilot begun on borrowed energy, staffed by an unplowed leadership, abandoned in the dip, leaving the congregation warier of the next real thing than it was before. A *not now* with a date attached is a faithful decision. A *no* with a clear conscience is a faithful decision.

And take one gift with you regardless: [the Guardrails](movement-ii-what-is-your-soil/03-the-guardrails.md). Those seven commitments about how a church holds what it knows of its people's hearts will bless any congregation that never runs a single pilot season. They are yours. The door stays open, and *not now* has never once meant *not ever* in the record of how God prepares a house.
