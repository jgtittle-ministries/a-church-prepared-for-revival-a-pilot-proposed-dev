# How These Foundations Were Tested

> **Draft status:** working draft (DEV) — offered for prayer and honest discussion.

A church weighing this proposal has a right to ask a blunt question: *did anyone ever seriously challenge this before it reached us?* Not "did the author believe it" — authors always believe it — but did anyone sit down with the foundations underneath this book and try, deliberately and at length, to break them?

The answer is yes. This chapter tells you how it was done, what held, what did not, and what is still unfinished. It is offered as testimony of diligence, not as a boast — and it begins with an honest explanation of what this testing was, and what it was not.

## What this testing was — and what it was not

Behind this book stand twelve foundation documents — the papers in *Intentional Journey of the Heart* that work out the convictions this proposal rests on: that ordinary believers can hear God and obey Him, that formation of the heart can be shepherded and even honestly observed, and that revival rises and drains away in recognizable patterns.

Each of those twelve documents was examined through a structured internal review method. Here is how it worked, stated plainly. For each document, a panel of reviewer-personas was assembled — each one *written in the voice* of a trusted figure whose lifework bears directly on the questions at stake: historical shepherds who actually kept watch over souls, theologians who thought hard about the church, measurement scientists who built and criticized instruments, and clinicians who spent careers with the wounded. Each persona was reconstructed from that figure's published work, and asked to challenge the document as that tradition would challenge it — with its hardest questions, not its polite ones. Then a separate adversarial verification pass took every finding the panel produced and tried to refute it against the document's actual text, page by page. Only findings that survived that hunt were counted.

Now the part that must be said without any softening, because a church deserves total clarity here: **these were exercises in perspective.** They were designed to force the hardest questions each tradition would ask — nothing more, and nothing less. No living or historical person actually reviewed, approved, or endorsed any of these materials, and none should be understood to have done so. When this chapter says a review "in the voice of" a Puritan pastor or a trauma clinician found something, it means the discipline of asking, from within that person's published thought, the question that person would most likely press — and then checking whether the document could answer it. This is not peer review in the academic sense, and it does not pretend to be. (One of the foundation papers has separately been under review at an academic journal, through the ordinary blind process; that is a different track, and it is not what this chapter describes.)

Why do it this way at all? Because a project written largely by one hand needs adversaries, and the best adversaries available were the great traditions themselves — the pastoral tradition that knows what oversight becomes when it curdles, the measurement tradition that knows what happens to any indicator once institutions start acting on it, the clinical tradition that has sat with the casualties of religious systems, the missionary tradition that asks what any of this is *for*. Writing their questions honestly, and then trying to refute the answers against the text, turned out to be a demanding exam. Whether it was demanding enough, you will be able to judge for yourself by the end of this chapter — because the results were not uniformly flattering.

## How the reviews worked

The process ran one document at a time, twelve documents in all, over many weeks.

For each document, the panel was matched to the material — a paper on measuring spiritual maturity drew measurement scientists, pastors who kept real records of real souls, and a clinician whose lens is power and the protection of the vulnerable; a paper on formation drew voices from monastic, Wesleyan, and contemporary formation practice. Each panel member wrote an independent review. Challenges were graded by severity: some were judged foundational — meaning the document could not fix them with a paragraph, because something structural was missing — and others were smaller repairs.

Then came the step that gave the process its teeth. An adversarial verification pass took every finding — every criticism *and* every piece of praise — and hunted through the document's full text with the deliberate aim of refuting it. Criticisms that the text could actually answer were downgraded or struck. Praise that the text could not support was struck too. What remained was a list of findings that had survived an honest attempt to kill them.

Two features of the results are worth reporting before the substance, because they speak to whether the process was real. First, the panels converged: reviewers written from completely unrelated traditions — a seventeenth-century pastor, a twentieth-century measurement scientist, a modern trauma clinician — repeatedly arrived at the same finding by different roads, which is what genuine problems look like. Second, the process found real faults, including at least one plain misattribution in a foundation paper's use of its sources — the kind of concrete, checkable error that a rubber-stamp exercise would never surface.

## What held

The center of everything — this book included — is one conviction: **the ordinary believer can hear God's voice in Scripture and prayer and obey it.** Not special Christians. Not professionals. The person in the pew.

That conviction was placed before all twelve panels — drawn from measurement science, pastoral practice, clinical trauma care, missiology, formation history, and the theology of the church under persecution — and each was invited to break it. Twelve reviews, twelve different directions of attack, and twelve confirmations, without a single qualification. Every serious challenge the process produced landed on the scaffolding around the core — how you observe formation, who governs the knowledge, what happens at large scale — and none of them touched the core itself. The verification pass checked this specifically: the surviving criticisms not only leave the core standing, most of them quietly *depend* on it.

The final reviews went further, and this is worth a church's attention. Two of the closing assessments concluded, from opposite ends of the intellectual world, that this conviction is not merely sound but *load-bearing* and *protective*. Load-bearing: without it, nothing else in the project works — you cannot speak of forming hearts toward hearing God if no one can actually hear Him. Protective: believers who know the Shepherd's voice for themselves are harder to mislead, harder to manipulate, and harder to steal — which makes this conviction, of all things, a safeguard against the very abuses the reviews worried about most. Jesus said it first: *"the sheep follow him, for they know his voice. A stranger they will not follow"* (John 10:4–5, ESV).

So when this book tells you that hungry hearts in your congregation can learn to hear and obey God — the claim underneath every movement that follows — you should know that this claim was challenged twelve times, on purpose, by the hardest questions six or more traditions could pose, and it held every time.

## What the reviews demanded

Here is the part a rubber stamp would omit. The reviews did break something — repeatedly, and from independent directions. What they broke was the assumption that an honest diagnostic of souls can simply be handed to institutions without designing, in advance, for power, for consequence, for decay, and for the fall.

The single largest finding of the whole series was reached five separate times by five unrelated reviewer perspectives — clinical, scientific, pastoral, operational, and missionary — and it was this: whenever a church or a movement learns to see the state of its people's hearts, that knowledge becomes power, and the relationship between that knowledge and power must be *designed*, not left to good intentions. The pastoral tradition knows what oversight becomes when the one keeping the record no longer knows the faces. The measurement tradition knows that any indicator tied to rewards gets corrupted — people begin producing the appearance the measure asks for. The clinical tradition has sat with people wounded by churches that used spiritual language as a means of control. History supplies the sobering cases: movements with the best formation systems ever built watched them harden into membership cards, and a famous modern congregation that measured hundreds of thousands of souls could not hear the truth being told about its own founder.

Most of the guardrails chapter in Movement II of this book exists because the reviews insisted on it. Specifically, the reviews demanded:

- **That knowledge of souls never become a management tool.** Whatever a church learns about its people's hearts exists to serve those people — as a mirror the community holds up to itself — never as a lever leadership pulls.
- **That nothing spiritual be scored, and nothing be tied to standing.** No rating of anyone's walk with God, and no connection — ever — between what is discerned about a person's heart and their position, advancement, reputation, or resources. The moment spiritual perception carries consequences for standing, people stop being honest and start performing.
- **That consent govern everything.** No one's interior life is discussed, recorded, or reported without their knowledge and free agreement — and no one else testifies about a person's soul behind their back.
- **That a church test itself by the least powerful.** The reviews pressed two questions any congregation can ask itself tonight, and this book has adopted both. First: how are the least powerful among us actually treated — the ones with nothing to offer and no leverage? Second: what happened to the last person who told this church something it did not want to hear? A church that cannot answer those questions well is not ready to know more about its people's hearts than it knows now.

If the guardrails in Movement II ever strike you as excessive — too careful, too slow, too suspicious of good intentions — know that they were not born from timidity. They were born from a review process that kept finding, from five directions at once, what happens when they are missing.

## What remains open

Honesty requires this section. The reviews did not end with everything resolved; they ended with a working list, and the underlying project maintains that list openly rather than quietly filing it away.

The list includes, in plain terms: governance and accountability structures still to be built — who watches the watchers, who examines the examiners, and who can tell a founder or a beloved leader a hard truth and be heard; further safeguards still to be written — a full consent architecture, and trauma-aware care in anything that asks people about their interior lives, so that a wound is never mistaken for immaturity; and measurement questions still unanswered — where the line falls between what may be honestly observed and what must only ever be confessed, how a framework makes room for the fallen and the returning (confession, lament, and restoration, not just growth), and how a church's health is measured by its effect on the world outside its walls, not only by its own internal reproduction.

Some of these have answers drafted in the larger project and not yet published; some are genuine open questions that no one has answered yet. The project's own assessment at the close of the series was candid on this point: the foundations are sound, and the specifications for the walls that still need building are now written down. This book is itself part of the response — its guardrails, its insistence on consent, its refusal to score anything spiritual, and its preference for the small, relational scale where a shepherd still knows the faces all come directly off that list.

## Why this matters for your church

If your congregation considers this pilot, you are being asked to trust something with the tenderest material a church holds — its people's hearts. You deserved to know, before anything else, whether the foundations under the proposal were ever pressure-tested, and where the known soft spots are. Now you know both: the core conviction held under twelve deliberate attempts to break it, and the places that needed reinforcement were found, named, and — in this book — built into guardrails rather than buried.

None of this replaces your own testing. The process described here was an internal discipline, and it says so; the final examiners of this proposal are you — your elders, your leaders, your congregation, on your knees, with your Bibles open. *"Test everything; hold fast what is good"* (1 Thessalonians 5:21, ESV). This chapter exists so that when you test it, you will not be the first — and so you can aim your hardest questions at the places the earlier testing marked.
