# The Question

> **Draft status:** working draft (DEV) — offered for prayer and honest discussion.

Do you want a revival?

Before you answer — and most of us answer this question in about half a second — let this chapter make one request: answer it slowly. The question deserves a slow answer, because it is not the question it appears to be. It appears to be a question about God, and whether we would like more of Him, and of course we would; who would say otherwise from a church pew? But asked honestly, it turns out to be a question about *us* — about our comfort, our calendars, our control, and what we are actually willing to have rearranged. That question takes longer than half a second, and it should.

This book asked on its first page that both of its questions be taken "honestly, not rhetorically." Here is what that means for this one. A rhetorical question already contains its answer, and the asker is only waiting for you to say it. This question does not. There are real churches, led by faithful people, for whom the honest answer right now is *not yet* — and this chapter will treat that answer with respect, because Christ treats honest hearts with respect, and because a "yes" extracted by pressure is not a yes at all. Nothing here is designed to work you toward a foregone conclusion. Something here is designed to make sure that whatever you conclude, you concluded it with your eyes open.

So: slowly, then. What is actually being asked?

## What we mean by revival

By *revival* this book means something quite specific, and quite simple: **God moving among His people in ways we do not control.**

Every word of that is doing work. *God moving* — the thing itself is His act, not ours; no committee has ever voted one into existence, and no committee ever will. *Among His people* — not first a wave of outsiders converted, though that often follows, but first a church awakened: cold hearts warmed, quiet prayers rekindled, sin long tolerated suddenly intolerable, the Scriptures alive in the hand again. And *in ways we do not control* — at a pace we do not set, through people we did not schedule, with results we cannot take credit for and could not have produced. Jesus said it of the Spirit's whole manner of working: "The wind blows where it wishes, and you hear its sound, but you do not know where it comes from or where it goes. So it is with everyone who is born of the Spirit" (John 3:8, ESV throughout). Revival is that wind arriving over a congregation at once. You can hear it. You cannot steer it.

Scripture prays for exactly this, which is worth noticing: the longing is not a modern invention. "Will you not revive us again, that your people may rejoice in you?" (Psalm 85:6). Notice who is being revived — *your people*, believers, the already-gathered — and notice what the revival is *for*: not excitement, but joy in God Himself. Habakkuk prays the same way from the middle of a discouraging century: "O LORD, I have heard the report of you, and your work, O LORD, do I fear. In the midst of the years revive it; in the midst of the years make it known; in wrath remember mercy" (Habakkuk 3:2). He has *heard the report* — he knows what God has done before — and he asks for it again, in his own ordinary, unspectacular years. That is the prayer this book is circling: Lord, do among us what the report says You have done.

And He has done it. The record is real. There have been seasons — in New England in the 1730s, in Wales in 1904, in the Hebrides, at Herrnhut, in a hundred smaller places that never made the histories — when God compressed into months what usually takes generations. Jonathan Edwards, a careful and rather unexcitable observer, counted more than three hundred souls brought in within about half a year, in a town of about eleven hundred people. Whole communities changed temperature. The next chapter of this movement walks through that record honestly — what actually happened, what lasted, what didn't, and why — so we will not spend it here. For now the point is only this: when this book says *revival*, it means the thing in that record. Not a metaphor. Not a mood. The thing itself.

## What we do not mean

Because the word has been stretched over so many other things, it is worth being plain about what this book does *not* mean.

We do not mean **an event series**. In much of American church life, "revival" has become a word for a scheduled week — dates on a banner, a guest speaker, special music. There is nothing wrong with special meetings; God has used them. But notice what the scheduling quietly claims: that the thing can be booked. A revival you can put on the calendar for the third week of October is, by the definition above, not the thing we are asking about. The wind does not take reservations.

We do not mean **an emotional weekend**. Genuine moves of God are full of feeling — how could they not be? Real people waking up to a real God will weep, and sing, and stay too late. But the feeling is the *weather* of revival, not the substance of it. It is entirely possible to manufacture the weather — skilled hands have done it with lighting and music and momentum for two centuries — and produce a room full of moved people and not one changed one. A stirring weekend that leaves the church's actual life untouched by November was an experience, and perhaps a good one. It was not a revival.

And we do not mean **nostalgia**. For some churches, "revival" is a word for the photograph on the fellowship-hall wall — the year the building was full, the beloved pastor, the summer the youth group caught fire. The longing in that photograph is not wrong; it may be the truest thing in the building. But a revival is not the past coming back. When God moves again, He will move among the people you actually are now, toward the future He actually intends, and the result will not look like the photograph. Churches that want the photograph back have already told God the terms — and setting terms is precisely what the definition rules out.

Strip those three away and you can feel how much smaller — and how much larger — the real question becomes. Smaller, because there is nothing to organize. Larger, because what remains is a living God, unmanaged, moving through your congregation at His own discretion. Which brings us to the part of the question most of us skip.

## Why the wanting has to be allowed to cost something

Here is a hard saying, offered pastorally: a want that has not been allowed to cost anything does not yet count as a want. It is an approval, a vote in favor, a warm opinion. Jesus made this a principle for anyone considering anything serious: "For which of you, desiring to build a tower, does not first sit down and count the cost, whether he has enough to complete it?" (Luke 14:28). Notice that counting the cost is not the opposite of desiring — it is what desiring does when it is sincere. So before your church says it wants a revival, it owes itself an honest sit-down over what revivals have actually done to the churches that received them.

They rearranged them. Gently sometimes, and sometimes not, but always. Three rearrangements recur in the record, and they land exactly where churches are tenderest.

**Comfort.** A revived congregation stops being a comfortable one, first of all about sin. When God draws near, the things we had made peace with stop being peaceful — old grudges, quiet dishonesty, the arrangement everyone knew about and no one named. In the accounts, repentance is not a theme of the meetings; it is the weather of the whole town — restitution paid, enemies reconciled, secret things brought into light. That is glorious to read about and genuinely painful to undergo. Isaiah says the reviving God dwells in two places: "the high and holy place, and also with him who is of a contrite and lowly spirit, to revive the spirit of the lowly, and to revive the heart of the contrite" (Isaiah 57:15). Contrition is the address revival gets delivered to. There is no other.

**Calendars.** God's timing in the record shows no interest in ours. Prayer meetings that were meant to end at nine did not end at nine. Work schedules, meal times, the whole liturgy of a well-run week bent around what God was doing — for months. A church whose real (if unstated) requirement is that God move within the existing schedule has, again, set terms. Revival keeps its own hours, and the churches that received one let it.

**Control.** This is the deepest one, and it is aimed at the leaders reading this. In the record, revival persistently arrives through the "wrong" people — praying grandmothers, teenagers, unpolished laymen — and persistently declines to route itself through the existing program. The pastors who fared best in revivals were the ones willing to become stewards of something they did not start and could not direct: correcting real excesses, yes, protecting the flock, yes, but fundamentally *keeping up with* God rather than presiding over Him. For a leader whose identity is competence, this is the costliest line in the whole ledger. It must be counted.

Say it plainly, then: to want revival is to want *being rearranged* — comfort, calendar, and control — by Someone who will not submit His plans for approval. If that sentence produces a gulp, good. The gulp is the question being asked honestly for the first time. And be encouraged: the gulp is not disqualifying. Nobody in Scripture met the living God casually. The point of counting the cost is not to talk you out of the tower. It is to make sure that if you build, you finish.

## Wanting revival, and wanting the feeling of revival

Now a finer distinction, and in some ways a more searching one, because it runs down the middle of sincere hearts — including, the writer would gently note, the hearts of people who love revival accounts enough to read books like this one.

There is a difference between wanting revival and wanting *the feeling* of revival. The feeling is real and it is wonderful: the electric room, the unhurried worship, the sense — so rare and so missed — of God unmistakably present. Once felt, or even once read about, it is longed for. But the feeling can be wanted the way any strong experience is wanted, for what it does *in us* rather than for Who it is. And the two wants, though they can look identical on Sunday, part ways under pressure. The church that wants revival will keep seeking God through the long, dry, unspectacular seasons — because it wants *Him*, and He is present in the dry seasons too. The church that wants the feeling will chase whatever seems to promise the sensation — the next conference, the next method, the next worship style — and grow quietly resentful of ordinary Sundays, as if God had stopped attending.

Here is a simple test, and it fits in one question: *would you still want what you are asking for if it came without the atmosphere?* If God moved in your church over three slow years instead of three astonishing weeks — deep repentance, restored marriages, recovered prayer, people hearing His voice in Scripture and obeying at real cost, and none of it electric, none of it reportable, nothing to film — would that satisfy the longing, or disappoint it? Be honest. The answer sorts the two wants completely. And take comfort in this: God is kind to both wants, because both began as hunger for Him. But only one of them will still be seeking Him in year three — and the record says year three usually matters more than week three.

## The wrong reasons

One more honesty, and then freedom.

It is possible to want revival — the real thing, cost counted — for reasons that quietly poison the wanting. Two are common enough to name in an opening chapter.

The first is **numbers**. The congregation has been shrinking; the budget follows attendance; revival would fill the room. And it would. But listen to what the wanting has become: God's visitation valued as a growth strategy — the Holy One of Israel welcomed for what He can do for our metrics. A church can slide into this without one cynical thought, simply by being anxious. The second is **reputation**. Revival at *our* church — the story travels, the denomination notices, the pastor is asked to speak on how it happened. Again, no one plans this. It arrives on its own, wearing the face of gratitude.

Why does it matter, if God gets sought either way? Because the wrong reason does not stay quiet. A church that wanted revival for the numbers will start protecting the numbers — softening the repentance that makes visitors uncomfortable, managing the movement to keep it marketable. A church that wanted it for reputation will start performing — and a performed revival is one of the specific ways the fire goes out, as the last movement of this book will show in detail. Wanting revival for the wrong reasons is not merely an impurity; it is a *failure mode*, and Movement IV catalogs it among the others. This chapter only posts the warning at the door: before you ask God to move, ask why you want Him to. He already knows. The examination is for you.

The prophets' word to a revival-seeking people, after all, began underground: "Break up your fallow ground, for it is the time to seek the LORD" (Hosea 10:12). Fallow ground is ground with a crust on it. Motives are part of the crust.

## The honest "not yet" — and what it costs

Now the freedom this chapter promised.

After all of the above, some readers — perhaps a pastor, reading alone, with a truer picture of the congregation than anyone else has — will conclude: *we are not ready to want this yet.* The grief is too fresh, the conflict too raw, the trust too thin; or simply, honestly, the leaders cannot yet say the yes and mean it.

Hear this clearly: **"not yet" is an honest answer, and this book will not shame you for it.** It may even be the most spiritually serious answer in the room — the answer of someone who counted the cost and refused to pretend. A yes that is really a performance helps no one, least of all God, who "desires truth in the inward being" (Psalm 51:6). If "not yet" is the truth, say it, and say it to Him. He has heard it before. He is remarkably patient with people who tell Him the truth.

But an honest "not yet" deserves an honest ledger, so let the cost of waiting be counted too — because waiting is not free, and here is why: **the drift downward does not pause while we decide.** This book's first page named two convictions, and the second was that spiritual life — in a soul, in a family, in a congregation — runs downhill by default, silently, on no one's decision. A church is never simply holding its position. The current is always moving, and it moves in one direction. So "not yet" does not mean *we will stay where we are until we are ready*. It means *we will be somewhat further downstream when we take this up again* — a little cooler, a little more settled into the forms, a little harder to wake. Not damned; not lost; just further. The full anatomy of that current — how it works, why it is nobody's fault, and how it is reversed — waits in [The Drift Downward](reader.html#docs/movement-iv-keeping-what-comes/01-the-drift-downward.md), and a leadership team leaning toward "not yet" would do well to read that chapter *before* settling the answer, not after.

And one more entry in the ledger, gentler but real: "times of refreshing... come from the presence of the Lord" (Acts 3:20) — and Peter names what precedes them: "Repent therefore, and turn back" (Acts 3:19). The refreshing has a direction it comes from. A church that waits without turning is not actually waiting for revival; it is only waiting. Let "not yet," if it must be said, at least be said facing the right way.

## Free, and invited

So the question stands, asked now with its full weight: **do you want a revival?** Not the event series, not the weekend, not the photograph — God Himself moving among you, uncontrolled, rearranging comfort and calendar and control, at His pace, for His glory, with your church's deepest sicknesses called up into the light and healed. Wanted not for the numbers and not for the name, but for Him. That thing. Do you want it?

You are free. Free to say *yes*, soberly, cost counted. Free to say *not yet*, honestly, ledger open, facing the right way. And free to say the third answer, the one this chapter suspects is the truest one in most rooms: *I want to want it.* If that is your answer, take heart — you are in the oldest and best company. "I believe; help my unbelief!" (Mark 9:24) was a prayer Jesus answered on the spot, and *I want to want You* is a prayer of exactly that family. God has done more with less. Hunger for hunger is still hunger, and He has never turned it away.

Whichever answer you carry out of this chapter, do not carry it alone, and do not carry it far without checking it against the only Reviewer whose opinion finally matters. Because here is the remarkable thing waiting in the next chapter: the question this book has just asked your church is not, in the end, this book's question. Christ Himself has already put searching questions to real congregations — seven of them, by name, in letters that are still in your Bible — churches praised, churches warned, and one church that had everything except its first love. Before you settle what you want, it is worth a long look at what He asked of them, and how He asked it. That is where we go next: [The Seven Letters](reader.html#docs/movement-i-do-you-want-a-revival/02-the-seven-letters.md).

He asked in love then. He asks in love still.
