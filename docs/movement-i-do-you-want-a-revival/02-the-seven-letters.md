# The Seven Letters: What Christ Says to a Church

> **Draft status:** working draft (DEV) — offered for prayer and honest discussion.

This book opened with a question: do you want a revival? It would be fair to wonder whether that question is a device — a writer's way of getting your attention before the real material starts.

It is not a device, and here is the reason. The question has already been asked — in substance, if not in those words. Not by an author, not by a conference speaker, not by a denominational survey — by the risen Christ, in writing, to seven real congregations, and the letters are still in your Bible. Revelation chapters 2 and 3 contain seven short letters, dictated by the risen Lord to His servant John, each addressed to a particular church in a particular city in Roman Asia — Ephesus, Smyrna, Pergamum, Thyatira, Sardis, Philadelphia, Laodicea. Real places. Real congregations, with real reputations, real pressures, and real problems, some of which they knew about and some of which they did not.

These letters are the closest thing we have to hearing Christ conduct a church review. Before this book asks anything of you, it wants you to sit with what He asked of them.

## He writes to churches, not only to Christians

The first thing to notice about the letters is easy to miss because we are so used to reading the Bible as individuals: each letter is addressed to a *congregation*, and it speaks to that congregation as one.

"I have this against you, that you have abandoned the love you had at first" (Revelation 2:4). That sentence is not addressed to a person. It is addressed to the church at Ephesus — the whole body, as a body. In the original language the "you" is singular: Christ speaks to the congregation the way you would speak to one soul. A church, in these letters, has works. It has a reputation. It has a temperature. It has a history it is accountable for — "remember therefore from where you have fallen" — and a future that depends on what it does next. Christ treats each congregation as something that can be praised, warned, and held responsible *as itself*, not merely as a container full of separate believers.

This should not entirely surprise us, because it is how God has always spoken. The prophets addressed whole cities and nations as moral agents — Amos read out indictments against nation after nation; Nineveh repented *as a city* under Jonah's preaching. Jesus did the same in His earthly ministry: He pronounced woes on Chorazin and Bethsaida as towns, and He wept over Jerusalem as one being with one will — "and you were not willing!" (Matthew 23:37). The seven letters are not an oddity. They are the risen Lord continuing a practice that runs through the whole of Scripture: God deals with His people not only one by one but *together* — and He holds the together accountable.

And yet — hold this alongside it, because the letters hold both — every one of the seven letters ends by turning to the individual. "He who has an ear, let him hear what the Spirit says to the churches" (Revelation 2:7). Each letter closes with a promise not to the church but to "the one who conquers." The diagnosis is corporate; the response is personal. No one in Sardis could hide behind the congregation's deadness, and no one in Sardis was doomed by it either — "you have still a few names in Sardis, people who have not soiled their garments" (Revelation 3:4). A faithful person can walk in white inside a dying church. Christ never lets the church's condition override a single willing heart.

That double address matters for everything this book will ask later. When we talk about the spiritual condition of a congregation, we are not inventing a category. We are standing on ground the Lord Himself staked out: churches have conditions, Christ reads them accurately, and He says so — while leaving every individual member free to hear and respond.

## How He examines: praise, diagnosis, prescription, promise

The second thing to notice is *how* Christ goes about it. The seven letters are not seven moods. They follow a form — deliberate, repeated, and worth learning from, because it is the pattern of a physician, not a prosecutor.

Each letter opens with Christ presenting Himself — and here is a detail that rewards attention: He introduces Himself differently to each church, and the introduction matches the diagnosis. To Ephesus, whose lampstand is in danger, He is the one who walks among the lampstands. To Smyrna, facing prison and death, He is the one "who died and came to life" (Revelation 2:8). To Sardis, which is spiritually dead, He comes as the one who holds the sevenfold Spirit of God — the very life the church lacks. To self-satisfied Laodicea, He is "the faithful and true witness" (Revelation 3:14) — the one whose testimony about them can be trusted, since their testimony about themselves cannot. Before He says a word of assessment, He has already shown each church exactly the facet of Himself its condition requires.

Then, seven times, the same two words: "I know." I know your works. I know your tribulation. I know where you dwell. This is the hinge of every letter. The examination is not a questionnaire the church fills out; it is the perfect knowledge of the One with eyes "like a flame of fire" (Revelation 2:18). Whatever else these letters are, they are not based on self-report.

Then — and lose this and you lose the heart of the method — *praise comes first, wherever praise is possible*. Ephesus, about to receive the most famous rebuke in the New Testament, is first commended at length: "I know your works, your toil and your patient endurance" (Revelation 2:2). Thyatira, harboring serious corruption, first hears that "your latter works exceed the first" (Revelation 2:19). Christ is scrupulously fair. He does not let a church's failure erase its faithfulness, and He does not let its faithfulness excuse its failure. Two churches — Smyrna and Philadelphia — receive no rebuke at all. One church — Laodicea — receives no praise at all. The other four are weighed honestly — though Sardis's only praise is for "a few names" of faithful individuals within it, never for the church itself.

Then the diagnosis, and notice how *focused* it is. "But I have this against you" — this, singular. One thing. Ephesus has abandoned its first love. Thyatira tolerates Jezebel. Pergamum's "a few things" turn out to be one disease in two forms. Christ does not bury a church under a list of twenty deficiencies. He names the one thing that is killing it. Anyone who has sat through a church assessment that produced forty action items may feel the difference. The Great Physician practices a severe economy: find the wound, name the wound, treat the wound.

Then the prescription — and the prescriptions differ, because the diseases differ. The two healthy churches are not given corrections; they are told to *hold fast* what they have. The sick churches each get medicine mixed for their sickness: Ephesus must remember, repent, and do the first works; Sardis must wake up and strengthen what remains; Laodicea must come to Christ and buy what it thinks it already owns. There is no all-purpose program in these letters. There is a Lord who knows each church and treats each church.

And then, every time, a promise — "to the one who conquers," a promise reaching all the way to the end of the book: the tree of life, escape from the second death, a new name, a place as a pillar in the temple of God, a seat with Christ on His throne. The letters that begin with lampstands under examination end with the promise of a city that needs no lamp at all, for "its lamp is the Lamb" (Revelation 21:23, ESV throughout).

Praise where praise is due. Diagnosis, exact and singular. Prescription matched to the disease. Promise out of all proportion to the pain. If a leadership team learned nothing else from Revelation 2–3 than to speak to their own congregation this way, the study would repay itself.

## The church that had everything

Of the seven, one letter belongs at the center of a book about revival, because it describes the church most of us would join tomorrow.

Ephesus was, by every visible measure, a superb church. Read the commendation slowly: works, toil, patient endurance. Intolerance of evil. Doctrinal vigilance — they had "tested those who call themselves apostles and are not, and found them to be false" (Revelation 2:2). Perseverance under pressure without growing weary. This was a hardworking, discerning, orthodox, durable congregation — a church that guarded the truth and paid its costs. If Ephesus were in your town, it would have a reputation for seriousness. Its statement of faith would be immaculate.

"But I have this against you, that you have abandoned the love you had at first."

Everything intact except the fire it was built to hold. The works continued; the love that once produced the works had gone out of them. And notice the verb — *abandoned*, not *lost*. You lose your keys. You abandon a post. Christ describes the cooling of first love not as a misfortune that befell the church but as something the church did — which is, if you will receive it, the most hopeful word in the letter, because what was abandoned can be returned to.

Then comes the sentence that ought to keep every elder board honest: "If not, I will come to you and remove your lampstand from its place, unless you repent" (Revelation 2:5). The lampstand is the church itself — "the seven lampstands are the seven churches" (Revelation 1:20). Christ is saying that a congregation can be removed *as a light* while, presumably, its activities continue. The building open, the services running, the budget met — and the lampstand gone. A church can die as a church before it dies as an organization. Sardis, two letters later, is the proof that this is possible: "You have the reputation of being alive, but you are dead" (Revelation 3:1). Sardis's letter mentions no persecution, no heresy — so far as Christ names any cause, nothing dramatic had happened to Sardis at all. That was the diagnosis.

And the remedy Christ gives Ephesus is startlingly practical. "Remember therefore from where you have fallen; repent, and do the works you did at first." Remember — not nostalgically, but honestly: measure the distance between what you were and what you are. Repent — call the cooling what it is. And then, *do the first works* — not "feel the first feelings." Christ prescribes behavior. First love is recovered the way it was first expressed: by doing again the things love did. Any church that has said "we can't manufacture the old passion" is quite right — and has not yet heard the prescription, which never asks for manufactured passion. It asks for first works, and entrusts the fire to God.

If revival means anything like what this book means by it — God restoring first love and first works to a people — then the letter to Ephesus is the case that proves the question is real. Here is Christ Himself telling a fully functioning church that it needs what it lost at the beginning, telling it exactly how to seek that, and telling it plainly what is at stake if it will not.

## What the letters do to our measurements

Now stand back from the seven letters and look at them as a set, and something uncomfortable comes into view.

The two churches that receive *no rebuke at all* — Smyrna and Philadelphia — are the two that look weakest. Smyrna is poor and persecuted: "I know your tribulation and your poverty (but you are rich)" (Revelation 2:9). Philadelphia has "but little power" (Revelation 3:8). No mention of size, growth, influence, or resources — because they had none to mention. By nearly every measure a modern church-health survey would apply, these two congregations would score at the bottom. Christ gives them the only two clean reports in Asia.

And the church with the *best* self-assessment gets the worst letter of the seven. Laodicea's own report reads: "I am rich, I have prospered, and I need nothing." Christ's report reads: "you are wretched, pitiable, poor, blind, and naked" (Revelation 3:17). It is the only letter with no commendation of any kind, and its diagnosis is, precisely, a misdiagnosis — the church's real disease was the gap between its self-report and the truth. Laodicea did not need better data about its neighborhood. It needed to discover that its data about *itself* was wrong, and only the faithful and true Witness could tell it so.

A word here about a famous illustration, because honesty about our sources is part of preparing soil. For decades, preachers have explained the "lukewarm" image (Revelation 3:15–16) by Laodicea's water supply — tepid water arriving by aqueduct, contrasted with a neighboring city's hot springs and another's cold streams. It is vivid, and it is very likely wrong. Recent archaeological and textual work at the site indicates that Laodicea's water was in fact good — the record suggests the city was praised in its own day for its water — and the tidy hot-springs/cold-streams/tepid-pipes triangle does not hold up. We mention this not to score a point but because it is a small parable of this whole chapter: a plausible story about a church, repeated confidently for years, that did not survive examination. The image itself needs no aqueduct anyway, and its edge survives intact: hot is useful, cold is useful — either serves. Lukewarm serves nothing, and Christ says it turns His stomach. The opposite of a revived church, in this letter, is not a hostile church. It is a useless one that feels fine.

Put Smyrna, Philadelphia, and Laodicea side by side and the conclusion is unavoidable: *Christ's evaluation of a church is fully decoupled from its visible prosperity.* Poverty read as riches; wealth read as wretchedness; weakness commended; self-sufficiency spat out. This does not make numbers useless — later movements of this book will argue for honest measurement, carefully guarded. But it fixes, permanently, the order of authority. Every instrument we ever use runs *under* His assessment, never in place of it. The letters are the standing reminder that a church can pass every survey and fail the only review that matters — and can fail every survey and hear "but you are rich."

## He gives time

If the letters were only diagnosis, they would be terrifying. But threaded through them is a pattern of patience that runs the whole length of Scripture: God diagnoses, warns, and then *gives time*.

You can watch the sequence in the letter to Thyatira. Concerning the false prophetess the church was tolerating, Christ says: "I gave her time to repent, but she refuses to repent" (Revelation 2:21). Time was given — a real probation, now expired for her, still open for those she misled. It is the same pattern Jesus told as a parable: the fig tree that bore no fruit, and the vinedresser who pleads, "Sir, let it alone this year also, until I dig around it and put on manure" (Luke 13:8). Notice that the extra year is not idle waiting — it is a year of digging and fertilizing, active cultivation with a deadline. When Christ warns a church, the warning *is* the mercy. Judgment announced is judgment delayed on purpose, so that repentance can happen.

Which raises the natural question: did they repent? What became of the seven churches? Here the honest answer is that history is not a laboratory, and we should hold what the record shows loosely. But the record suggests something better than fatalism. Sardis — "dead" Sardis — produced, in the following century, one of the most respected Christian teachers in all of Asia. Ephesus, warned that its lampstand could be removed, did not wink out; it went on to become a leading center of Christianity in that part of the world for centuries. If the letters had been fixed predictions, that would be a puzzle. But they were never predictions. Every threat in them carries an "unless," spoken or implied — "unless you repent." A warning that is heeded leaves exactly this kind of evidence: the disaster that didn't happen, the dead church that got up. The record suggests, at least in some of these cities, that the letters *worked* — which is, after all, what letters like these are for.

That is worth saying directly to any leadership team reading its church's condition with a sinking heart: in these letters, no diagnosis is a death sentence. The church told it was dead was also told to strengthen what remained — which means something remained. The church facing removal was handed a three-step path home. Even Laodicea, the one church with nothing to commend, received the tenderest invitation in the whole set. The time you are living in right now — this year, with its ordinary Sundays — may be precisely the vinedresser's year: given, on purpose, for digging.

## Whom He loves, He reproves

One more feature of the letters, and it changes how a church should hear everything above.

The harshest letter — Laodicea's, the letter with zero praise, the letter with "I will spit you out of my mouth" — contains these two sentences, nearly back to back: "Those whom I love, I reprove and discipline, so be zealous and repent" (Revelation 3:19), and then, "Behold, I stand at the door and knock. If anyone hears my voice and opens the door, I will come in to him and eat with him, and he with me" (Revelation 3:20). We usually lift that second verse out for evangelism, and it serves there. But read it where it sits: it is spoken to the worst church of the seven. The most scathing congregational review in Scripture ends with Christ outside the door of that very congregation, knocking, asking to come in and share a table.

This is the temperature of the whole exercise. Christ does not diagnose churches the way an inspector condemns a building. He diagnoses them the way a father tells a grown child the truth — because He loves them and wants them back. The severity and the tenderness are not in tension; the severity *is* the tenderness, refusing to let a church it loves sleepwalk into losing its lamp. Any use we ever make of these letters — and this book is about to propose one — has to keep that temperature. The moment congregational self-examination becomes shaming, scorekeeping, or a weapon in a church fight, it has left the spirit of Revelation 2–3 entirely. He reproves because He loves. So must we, or not at all.

## If He wrote to us this year

Now the mirror.

From very early in church history, readers noticed that these seven letters were never meant to stop at seven addresses. Each one ends with the same widening formula — "let him hear what the Spirit says to the churches," plural. Every church was meant to read all seven letters — including, pointedly, the six not addressed to it. Early Christian readers said as much: what He says to one, He says to all. Seven, in this book of sevens, has the flavor of completeness. The letters read like a spectrum of the conditions a congregation can be in — which is why, twenty centuries later, they still read like they were opened in our mailbox by mistake.

So here is the question this chapter has been building toward, and it is the question "do you want a revival?" wearing its most concrete clothes:

**If Christ wrote a letter to our church this year, which of the seven would it most resemble?**

Would it read like Ephesus — sound, hardworking, discerning, and cooler at the core than anyone wants to admit? Like Smyrna — pressed, poor, and rich? Like Pergamum or Thyatira — faithful in much, but tolerating something that should have been dealt with long ago? Like Sardis — a reputation running on momentum? Like Philadelphia — small, out of breath, and holding fast? Like Laodicea — comfortable, well-resourced, and needing nothing?

A leadership team can ask this question together, and the letters themselves show the honest way to do it:

*Read all seven letters aloud, together, before discussing any of them.* Every church in Asia read all seven case files; so should you. It is remarkably harder to self-flatter with all seven open on the table.

*Praise first, the way He does.* Before anyone names a concern, name what is genuinely commendable in your congregation — the toil, the endurance, the love that is really there. Christ never skips this step, and teams that skip it produce despair, not repentance.

*Then let each person answer privately before anyone answers publicly.* Which letter is ours, and in one sentence, why? Written down, unsigned if need be. The Laodicea trap is a shared self-report that no one individually believes; private answers get past the official story.

*Then compare — and stay in "I have this against you" discipline.* One thing. Not a forty-item list; the one thing that, if Christ named it, would explain the most. If your team's answers cluster, you have probably found your letter. If they scatter, that too is information — it may mean the honest diagnosis needs more than an evening, which is exactly where the next movement of this book is headed.

*And end with the promise, not the diagnosis.* Every letter does. Whatever your church's condition, the letters insist on two things at once: that Christ sees it exactly as it is, and that He is standing closer to the door than you thought.

This exercise is not yet a diagnosis of your soil — that is Movement II's work, and it needs more care, more voices, and real guardrails. But it is the right first look in the mirror, because the mirror belongs to Him. The question "do you want a revival?" turns out not to be a question this book is putting to your church. It is a question the risen Christ has already put to churches — by name, in love, with a path home attached. The only thing this chapter adds is the observation that He has not changed, and churches have not either.

He who has an ear, let him hear.
