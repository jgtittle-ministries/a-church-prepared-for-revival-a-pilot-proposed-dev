# Hungry Hearts

> **Draft status:** working draft (DEV) — offered for prayer and honest discussion.

Everything in this movement has been building to one question. We have looked at the four soils. We have admitted that a congregation is not simply the average of its members, and that the counters — attendance, giving, participation — cannot see what we most need to see. All of that was preparation for this:

**Does our soil include hearts that are hungry for more of God?**

Not "do we have committed members." Not "is our attendance healthy." Not "are people serving." Those questions have their place, and every church already knows how to ask them. This question is different, and most churches have never asked it out loud: *somewhere in our pews — maybe third row on the left, maybe running the sound board, maybe teaching the four-year-olds — are there people who are quietly, privately hungry for more of God than they are currently getting?*

Here is why the question matters so much. Every revival in the record began with hungry people. Not with programs, not with campaigns — with a handful of hearts that ached for God and started seeking Him like people who mean it. The hunger came first; the rain found the hunger. And Jesus made hunger itself a promise: *"Blessed are those who hunger and thirst for righteousness, for they shall be satisfied"* (Matthew 5:6, ESV). Notice what He blesses. Not the full. Not the satisfied. The hungry. If your congregation contains hungry hearts, you are already holding the most precious thing a preparing church can hold — and you probably do not know who they are.

This chapter is about how to find out. And — this is the part that makes it different from every survey you have ever run — how the very act of finding out becomes the first step of feeding them.

## What hunger sounds like in an ordinary pew

Start by learning to recognize the thing itself, because spiritual hunger almost never announces itself in spiritual language. Nobody stands up in the announcements and says, "I am starving for God." Hunger in an ordinary pew sounds like this:

**It sounds like longing.** A woman who has been in the church thirty years says, almost apologetically, "I read my Bible, I pray, I come every week — but I remember when it felt *alive*, and I miss that." She is not complaining about the church. She is homesick for God.

**It sounds like restlessness.** A man in his forties who serves on two committees says, "I don't know. I keep thinking I should be doing something more — not busier. More. Does that make sense?" It makes perfect sense. Restlessness of that particular kind is often desire that has not yet found its name.

**It sounds like the sentence "there must be more than Sunday."** Sometimes it is said with a laugh, sometimes with an edge, sometimes with tears that surprise the person crying them. A teenager says it about youth group. A grandmother says it about the whole shape of her spiritual life. When you hear that sentence — *there has to be more than this* — do not rush to defend Sunday. You may be listening to John 10:10 waking up in a person: Jesus said He came that they may have life, and have it abundantly, and something in this person has just noticed the gap between that promise and their experience. The gap hurts. The hurt is hunger.

**And it sounds like dryness that aches.** This one you must get right, because instinct will get it wrong. Some of your most spiritually hungry people currently *feel nothing* — prayer flat, Scripture flat, worship flat — and they have quietly concluded that the dryness means something is broken in them. Here is the thing your leaders must know: dryness that *aches* — where the person misses God, mourns the distance, keeps showing up in anguish — is very often not a sign of a heart going cold. It is frequently the sign of a heart very much alive, in a season God Himself is using. The drifting soul does not ache. The hungry one does. We give this a whole chapter later in the book, [When the Ground Goes Dry](reader.html#docs/movement-iii-preparing-the-soil/04-when-the-ground-goes-dry.md), because misreading dryness is the one pastoral mistake a preparing church cannot afford. For now, hold this much: when someone tells you their spiritual life has gone dry *and it hurts them*, you may be looking at one of the hungriest hearts in your congregation.

Longing, restlessness, "there must be more," dryness that aches. Four accents of one language. Now — how does a church actually listen for it? Not with a form. With a conversation. And for that conversation, we need a simple map of how a person connects with God at all.

## The Four Connects

The instrument this book proposes is called the **Four Connects**, and it comes out of more than twenty years of small-group formation work behind this proposal — work now piloted with families in [*Fellowship of the Heart*](https://jgtittle-ministries.github.io/fellowship-of-the-heart-pilot-at-cca/), with its foundations documented in [*Intentional Journey of the Heart*](https://jgtittle-ministries.github.io/Intentional-Journey-of-the-Heart/).

It began, as the best things do, with Scripture. One summer years ago, a group of believers was studying Matthew 22, where Jesus says the whole Law and the Prophets hang on two commandments: *"You shall love the Lord your God with all your heart and with all your soul and with all your mind"* — and, like it, *"You shall love your neighbor as yourself"* (Matthew 22:37–39, ESV). And they noticed something hiding in plain sight. Loving God with *all* your heart requires actually knowing what is in your heart. Loving your neighbor *as yourself* requires knowing your neighbor and being known by your neighbor. Both of those depend on a real connection with God Himself — not a category called "God" in our heads, but the living God who knows our names. And when those three begin to be real, something else shows up: a sense of what we are *for*. Works to walk into. The Bible calls them *"good works, which God prepared beforehand, that we should walk in them"* (Ephesians 2:10, ESV).

So there are four connections, and they form the whole map:

**Connecting with Self.** This is knowing what is actually inside me — my story, the real condition of my heart, my honest questions. Not the version of me I present on Sunday morning; the actual interior. Scripture commands this honesty and shows us how to seek it: *"Search me, O God, and know my heart! Try me and know my thoughts! And see if there be any grievous way in me"* (Psalm 139:23–24, ESV). Most of us are strangers to our own hearts — surprised by what we do, surprised by what comes out of our mouths — because, as Jesus taught, the mouth speaks from what fills the heart. Connecting with Self simply means letting God show me what is really in there: where my soil is hard, where it is rocky, where the thorns are, and where the good ground is. It is not self-absorption. It is the honesty that loving God with *all* my heart requires — I cannot give Him all of a heart I refuse to look at.

**Connecting with Others.** This is being truly known by at least one other person — and truly knowing them. Not having friends; most of us have friends. Having at least one person outside my own head who knows what is actually going on in my interior life: my real struggles, my real doubts, my real joys. *"Two are better than one… For if they fall, one will lift up his fellow. But woe to him who is alone when he falls"* (Ecclesiastes 4:9–10, ESV). Enormous numbers of faithful church members — including leaders — have no such person. They are surrounded weekly and known by nobody. Connecting with Others means stepping out of that hidden loneliness into real Christian community, where confession, encouragement, and honest speech are normal.

**Connecting with God.** This is the center: hearing Him — above all in Scripture — and learning the listening posture as a practice, the way you would learn any skill that matters. *"So faith comes from hearing, and hearing through the word of Christ"* (Romans 10:17, ESV). This book's deepest conviction, carried over from the longer work behind it, is that the ordinary believer can hear God's voice in Scripture and prayer and obey it — not the special Christians, not the mystics, the *ordinary* believer. Connecting with God means moving from reading the Bible as information to receiving it as address: coming to the page expecting the living God to speak, and being willing to obey what He says. Many people in your pews have experienced this in flashes — a verse that suddenly landed with their name on it — and have never been told that the flash can become a life.

**Connecting with Mission.** This is walking into the works God prepared for me to do (Ephesians 2:10 again — prepared *beforehand*; the works exist and are waiting). Not manufacturing a ministry out of guilt or ambition, but discovering the thing God has already shaped me for — the place where my gifts, my story, and His purposes meet, the work that makes me come alive rather than merely tired. Every believer has such works waiting. Most have never been helped to find them, and many carry a quiet grief about it: the sense of being *for* something they have never quite located.

Now, the crucial design fact, and it must not be flattened: **these four are not four topics on a list. They are a sequence, and the order matters.** Self, then Others, then God, then Mission — each connection making the next one possible. You cannot connect honestly with others until you have begun to be honest with yourself; you have nothing true to offer them. You cannot connect with God in a way that goes beyond performance until you are honest with yourself and have at least one person who knows your interior life; without those, the God-relationship tends to become another stage to perform on. And you cannot find and live the mission God prepared for you until the first three connections are real; mission built on an unconnected life becomes ambition wearing a name tag. The sequence is why so many sincere attempts at "more of God" stall: people try to leap straight to intimacy with God, or straight to world-changing mission, while skipping the honest self-knowledge and the honest community those things stand on.

That is the whole instrument. Four connections, in order, resting on the two great commandments. A layperson can learn it in ten minutes and carry it for life. And in this book's design, it does two jobs at once — which is where the hungry hearts come in.

## The first duty: a conversation that finds what no attendance sheet can see

Here is what the Four Connects make possible, and here is where this book asks you to trust something slower and more human than a metric.

You do not find hungry hearts with a survey. You find them the way Jesus found people — one conversation at a time. The Four Connects give an elder, a small-group leader, or a mature friend a gentle, natural shape for a conversation that would otherwise be hard to start. Over coffee, after the service, on a walk — with the person's willing consent, never as an ambush — it sounds like this:

*"Can I ask you something I've been asking a few people, just because I care about the answer? How connected do you feel — really — to your own heart these days? Is there anyone in your life who knows what's actually going on inside you? When you read Scripture or pray, does God ever feel present — does He ever say anything to you? And do you have a sense of what He's made you for?"*

Four questions. Fifteen minutes. No clipboard.

And watch what happens. The attendance sheet told you this man is a *committed member*: present forty-eight Sundays a year, tithes faithfully, serves on the property team. The conversation tells you something the sheet never could: that when you asked about connecting with God, he went quiet, and then said, "Honestly? I can't remember the last time any of it felt real. I keep waiting for somebody to ask me that." *There* is a hungry heart — invisible to every counter in the building, discovered in a quarter of an hour of loving attention.

This is the whole argument of the previous chapter made practical. The person who performs the behaviors and the person whose heart is being transformed look identical to every instrument that counts — and they sound completely different within five minutes of a real conversation about their four connects. Hunger shows up in the answers, and just as often in the pauses: the longing when she talks about a season years ago when God felt near; the flicker of pain when he admits nobody really knows him; the wistfulness — *"there's got to be more than this, right?"* — that slips out before the polished answer arrives. You are not scoring these conversations. You are listening for the ache. A shepherd does not need a spreadsheet to recognize a hungry sheep; he needs proximity, attention, and a question worth asking.

Notice, too, what the conversation does even when it finds *no* hunger — when the person is genuinely content, or politely deflects, or isn't interested. It has still done something almost no church interaction does: it has asked a human being about the actual state of their soul, with warmth and without an agenda. Nobody is harmed by having been lovingly asked. Many people will remember for years that someone asked.

## The second duty: the same conversation is the invitation

Now the design move that makes the Four Connects more than a diagnostic — the double duty this book was built around.

A survey ends when the data is collected. This conversation ends differently, because *the instrument is made of the path itself.* The four things you just asked about are not measurement categories — they are the four connections a person can actually begin to nurture, starting this week. Which means the moment hunger surfaces, the very framework that surfaced it is already in your hands to offer as a way forward. Diagnosis and invitation in one motion.

Concretely: the conversation uncovers the hunger, and then keeps going one more step —

If the ache showed up around **Self**: *"What if you spent ten minutes this week with Psalm 139 — 'Search me, O God, and know my heart' — and just wrote down, honestly, what's actually in there right now? Nobody sees it but you and Him."* A first step, this week, that costs nothing and starts everything.

If it showed up around **Others**: *"Is there one person you'd trust with one true sentence about how you're really doing? Not the whole story — one sentence, this week."* Being known starts that small.

If it showed up around **God**: *"Try this every morning for a week: before you pick up your phone, sit for five minutes with one question — 'Father, what are You up to today, and what do You want me to notice?' Then in the evening, write one sentence about what you noticed."* That practice — the simplest doorway into the listening posture there is — comes straight from the pilot fellowship's first week, and a twelve-year-old can do it. So can a seventy-year-old who has been in church her whole life and never once been taught to listen.

If it showed up around **Mission**: *"Here's a question to carry around this week: when you show up somewhere at your best — not performing, just fully yourself — what does the room get? Ask somebody who loves you what they'd say."* The works prepared beforehand usually sit closer to home, and further downhill, than people expect.

Each connect, in other words, is something a hungry person can begin feeding *immediately* — no program required, no waiting for the church to organize anything. And for the hearts that want more than a first step, the path continues: Movement III of this book proposes small fellowships built on exactly this frame — circles where the Four Connects are walked in order, in safe and honest community, over weeks — so that the hunger surfaced in one conversation has a real table to sit down at. The conversation is the doorway; the fellowship path is the house. A church doing this well never has to say "thanks for sharing, someone will follow up." It says: *"What you just described — that longing? There's a path for that, and it starts smaller than you think. Would you want company on it?"*

That sentence — *would you want company on it?* — is the entire method of this book in miniature. Not recruitment. Invitation. Hunger, met with a path and a companion.

## Never a form, never a score, never a report

One more thing must be said before this chapter closes, and it must be said plainly, because everything above it could be corrupted by ignoring it.

Everything described in this chapter happens in ordinary pastoral conversations — with consent, with warmth, between people who have or are building real relationship. It is a shepherd's practice, not an administrator's. The moment the Four Connects become a form that members fill out, a score entered in a spreadsheet, a dashboard reviewed at the elders' meeting, or a report on the state of anyone's soul passed up a chain — the instrument is not merely weakened. It is destroyed, and it becomes dangerous. A hungry heart that senses it is being *assessed* will close like a fist, and it will be right to. What was designed as a doorway becomes surveillance, and surveillance is the opposite of shepherding.

So let this stand as a rule wherever this proposal travels: **the Four Connects are a conversation, never an instrument of record. No forms. No scores. No reports.** What a leader learns in such a conversation is held *for* the person, never over them — held in prayer, held in follow-up, held in love, and held in confidence. The next chapter, *The Guardrails*, makes all of this binding and specific — who may know what, what consent means, and the one test every leadership must run on itself — and it is not optional reading. It sits beside every diagnostic in this book on purpose. If your church cannot commit to the guardrails, it should not ask these questions; better an undiscovered hungry heart than a betrayed one.

## A hungry heart discovered is a trust

Suppose your church does this. Suppose, over a season, a handful of leaders have a few dozen of these conversations — unhurried, warm, no clipboards — and suppose you discover what this book suspects you will discover: that your soil holds more hungry hearts than anyone guessed. The quiet woman who has been longing for years. The busy deacon whose service is hiding a drought. The teenager who thought she was the only one who felt that there must be more. Scattered through your ordinary pews: people already hungering and thirsting, already carrying the blessed condition of Matthew 5:6, waiting only to be found.

Understand what you will then be holding. A surfaced hunger is not a data point. It is a trust — one of the most delicate things a person can hand you. Someone has admitted, perhaps for the first time out loud, that they want more of God than they have. If the church that drew out that admission has nothing to offer it — no path, no table, no companions, just a warm conversation that leads nowhere — the hunger does not simply return to hiding. It returns wounded, and it learns not to speak again. The only thing worse than never finding your hungry hearts is finding them and then failing to feed them. *They shall be satisfied*, Jesus promised — and He is the one who does the satisfying; but He has always been pleased to set His table through His church.

Which is why this movement now ends at a threshold. You know what your soil is. You know how to find the hunger in it, and you know the guardrails that keep the finding holy. What remains is the question the hungry hearts themselves will ask you, and it is the question Movement III exists to answer: *you found us — now where is the food?* Preparing that table — starting with your own leaders, building the fellowship circles, readying the vessel before the rain — is Movement III's whole burden. Turn the page hungry.
