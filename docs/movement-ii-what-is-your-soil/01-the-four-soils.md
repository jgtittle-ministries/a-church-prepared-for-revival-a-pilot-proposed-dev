# The Four Soils

> **Draft status:** working draft (DEV) — offered for prayer and honest discussion.

Movement I ended with the risen Christ examining seven congregations and telling each one the truth about itself. If you have come this far, you have accepted the premise that a church *has* a condition — something Christ can read, praise, warn, and hold responsible as itself. Movement II asks the next question: can *we* read it? Not with His eyes — we will come to that limit before this chapter ends — but honestly and safely: can a church look at itself and see something true?

Jesus gave us the picture to look with. He told one story that He flagged as the key to all the others — "Do you not understand this parable? How then will you understand all the parables?" (Mark 4:13) — and it is a story about ground. A sower went out to sow. Some seed fell along the hard path, and the birds ate it. Some fell on rocky ground, sprang up fast, and withered in the sun for lack of root. Some fell among thorns, and the thorns choked it. And some fell on good soil and bore fruit — a hundredfold, sixty, thirty (Matthew 13:3–8). Then the words that aim the story at you: "He who has ears, let him hear" (Matthew 13:9).

The same sower. The same seed. Four outcomes — and the entire difference was the soil.

We usually read this parable one heart at a time, and we should; that is how Jesus explained it. But this book has been arguing since its first page that the parable also describes a congregation — that a church, taken whole, has a soil condition. This chapter walks the four soils at that scale. It is offered as a mirror. Please hear that word now, because everything here depends on it, and we will say it again before we are done: a mirror is something you stand in front of. It is not something you hold up to other people.

## The story Jesus told, and what He said it meant

Stay with the individual reading for a moment, because Jesus' own interpretation is the foundation for everything else. He explained each soil, and each explanation names not just *that* the word failed but *where* and *why*.

**The path.** "When anyone hears the word of the kingdom and does not understand it, the evil one comes and snatches away what has been sown in his heart" (Matthew 13:19). The seed never gets in. There is no crack in the surface. The word is heard, and nothing enters. Hearing happened; receiving did not.

**The rocky ground.** "This is the one who hears the word and immediately receives it with joy, yet he has no root in himself, but endures for a while, and when tribulation or persecution arises on account of the word, immediately he falls away" (Matthew 13:20–21). Notice how generous the description is: the reception is *immediate* and *joyful* — nothing about it fake. But the joy is fed from outside — novelty, belonging — and when the outside turns hostile, there is nothing inside to draw on. "No root in himself" is the diagnosis. The plant was real. It was just shallow.

**The thorns.** "This is the one who hears the word, but the cares of the world and the deceitfulness of riches choke the word, and it proves unfruitful" (Matthew 13:22). The thorns get their own section shortly, because the parable gives them a rank the church has rarely given them. For now, notice only this: of the three failures, the thorny ground is the only one where the word *lives*. It takes root. It grows. It is genuine. And it is slowly strangled by things that are not, in themselves, sins — cares and riches, worry and comfort. Luke widens the list: "they are choked by the cares and riches and pleasures of life, and their fruit does not mature" (Luke 8:14). Not killed. Choked. The plant is still standing there, green and fruitless.

**The good soil.** "This is the one who hears the word and understands it. He indeed bears fruit" (Matthew 13:23). Luke adds three qualities worth engraving somewhere: the good soil is those who, "hearing the word, hold it fast in an honest and good heart, and bear fruit with patience" (Luke 8:15). Hold it fast — the word is gripped, given priority when other things pull. An honest and good heart — the inside matches the outside; no performance gap. Fruit with patience — not a burst of enthusiasm but a harvest that takes seasons. Even here the yields differ, a hundredfold and sixty and thirty, which is the parable's quiet mercy: good soil is not a competition.

Read that way, the four soils are not four kinds of people, as though some of us were simply born pavement. They are four things that happen to the word — four points where it can be lost, and one way it is kept. That is what makes the parable a diagnostic instead of a verdict. Ground can be broken up; rocks dug out; thorns cleared. The prophet's command this book began with — "break up your fallow ground" (Hosea 10:12) — assumes exactly that: ground can change.

## A congregation has soil

Now widen the lens, and let the seven letters authorize the move. Christ addressed Ephesus, Sardis, and Laodicea each as *one* — one condition, one diagnosis, one "you." If a church can abandon its first love as a body, be dead as a body, be lukewarm as a body, then a church can be hard, shallow, thorny, or good ground as a body. The parable scales, because a congregation is a *system*, not a pile.

Here is the plainest way to see it. Take five genuinely mature believers — deep roots, honest hearts, real fruit — and put them in a room together weekly for a year. You would expect a mature group. You will not necessarily get one. Five individually mature people can form a group that avoids every hard conversation, keeps everything pleasant and shallow, and functions as a social hour with prayer attached — a spiritually immature *culture* made entirely of spiritually mature *members*. The maturity is present in the individuals but not in the space between them — and the space between them is where a congregation lives.

That is why a church's soil is not the average of its members' soil. Scripture never treats the body as arithmetic — "the body does not consist of one member but of many" (1 Corinthians 12:14) — and the "one another" commands that fill the New Testament are all addressed to the space between. A congregation's soil is its *shared culture*: what is normal here, what is rewarded here, what can be said out loud here, what happens to the word of God between the moment it is preached and the moment the parking lot empties. And the arithmetic runs both ways, which is the hopeful half: a congregation with rich relational soil can carry a struggling member's thin patch of ground for a season. Culture is stronger than headcount, in both directions.

So the question this chapter puts in your hands is the parable's question, asked of the whole: *when the word of God is sown here, week after week — what happens to it?*

## The hard path, at the scale of a church

Picture a church with excellent preaching, a full room, and no relational life underneath — no expectation that the word preached on Sunday will ever be spoken of again by the people who heard it. The seed is sown beautifully every week. It lands on a culture with no openings — polite, appreciative, sealed — and bounces. The preacher teaches; the members nod; everyone goes home unchanged, and the path gets a little harder from the foot traffic.

The hard-path church is easy to miss because it looks like success. But the parable is blunt about the difference between gathering and receiving: seed on the path is *heard* seed. Presence is not penetration. A congregation can attend faithfully for decades — can grow numerically the whole time — while the word never once enters its shared life.

One honest probe for path-hardness: when did the word last *cost* this congregation something? Not an individual member — the body. When did something preached here change a budget, end a program, start a hard conversation? Ground that nothing ever enters is ground that nothing ever disturbs.

## The rocky ground, at the scale of a church

The rocky-ground church is the most exciting church in town. High-energy services, a full calendar, events that draw crowds, newcomers greeted with genuine warmth, and joy — real joy, the parable insists on that. The word is received "immediately... with joy."

The question is what it is rooted in. In the rocky-ground congregation, the life of the body is fed from outside itself: the momentum of the program, the gifting of a magnetic leader, the pleasure of belonging to the thing that is working. Remove the external feed and you find out what was underneath. The pastor leaves, and the culture leaves with him. A painful conflict arrives — a moral failure, a betrayal, a season when the church's convictions become socially expensive — and the congregation that sang so loudly discovers it has "no root in itself." People who joined in eighteen months leave in eighteen months, some more cynical about the church than they came.

Here is a sobering test that costs nothing to run: if the most gifted leader in this church were gone for a season, would the congregation's spiritual life continue at depth — or would it reveal that the root system was in a person, not in the body? A church whose life is housed in one man's fire has warmth but no soil. In this parable it is the sun that kills.

## The thorns — the danger the parable ranks first

Now the section this chapter exists for.

Ask most church leaders to name the greatest threats to a congregation's spiritual life and the list comes back: secularism, persecution, false teaching, moral scandal. All real. But look at what Jesus put inside the parable. The path and the rocky ground describe the word failing to get established. The thorns are the only enemy in the story that defeats the word *after it has taken root* — the only thing shown overcoming a living, genuine work of God. And when Jesus names the thorns, He does not name persecution; the rocky ground already covered that. The thorns are "the cares of the world and the deceitfulness of riches" (Matthew 13:22) — and in Luke, "cares and riches and pleasures of life" (Luke 8:14).

Worry and wealth. Anxiety and comfort. Busyness and success. Not one item on that list is a scandal; not one would get anyone disciplined by an elder board. That is precisely what makes them deadly: thorns are not invaders, they are *residents*. They do not attack the plant; they just grow beside it, respectable and green, and take the light.

What do the thorns look like at the scale of a whole church? They look like a good church. Genuine community, warm relationships, real spiritual life — nothing about this congregation is fake. But the body has never ordered its shared life so that the kingdom actually comes first when things collide. The cares of the world, congregation-sized: the schedule pressure that makes every deeper commitment "a lot to ask right now"; the competing loyalties that mean the church gets everyone's margins and no one's core; the drift by which a fellowship becomes a support group, a social circle, a comfortable habit. The deceitfulness of riches, congregation-sized: the budget that flows to comfort and production while formation gets the leftovers; the quiet reign of the status quo in a body pleasant enough that nobody wants to risk spoiling it; success itself — attendance up, giving up, reputation up — whispering the deceit riches always whisper, *you have arrived, you may rest.* A thorny church is almost always a *nice* church. It can persist for years, meeting faithfully and sharing warmly, "and their fruit does not mature."

Laodicea sat at the end of Movement I for a reason. "I am rich, I have prospered, and I need nothing" (Revelation 3:17) is what the thorns sound like when they have finished their work: a church whose comfort grew up around the word until nothing could get through. If you lead a reasonably successful congregation in the modern West, this is the soil to fear. Hard paths and rocky ground announce themselves — emptiness and collapse are hard to miss. Thorns choke quietly, in the middle of what everyone agrees is a wonderful church. The parable put them first among the dangers to rooted things. So should we.

## The good soil, at the scale of a church

And what does Luke 8:15 look like when a whole congregation is the ground?

*Holding it fast* looks like a body where the word actually governs when things collide — where Scripture preached on Sunday shows up in Tuesday's decisions, where a budget line or a beloved program can lose an argument to the word of God. *An honest and good heart* looks like a congregation whose inside matches its outside — what is said from the platform and what is true in the hallways are the same thing, and struggle can be confessed without being punished. And *fruit with patience* looks like the most beautiful, least dramatic thing in church life: fruit nobody engineered. Leaders keep emerging without being recruited, because the culture grows them. Groups multiply without a campaign, because life spreads. Members carry the congregation's way of walking with God into their kitchens and workplaces without being sent by a program, because it is who they have become. In good soil, fruit is not produced. It grows.

Two cautions, so the good soil does not become a new burden. First, *with patience* means seasons, not quarters — a good-soil church can look unremarkable for a long time, the way a field in April looks like dirt. Second, the yields differ — a hundredfold, sixty, thirty — and Jesus counts all three as good soil. The parable measures faithfulness of ground, not size of harvest — and of the seven churches in Asia, the two that received no rebuke were the two with the least to count.

## The limits of counting

Every congregation counts something — attendance, giving, baptisms, group sign-ups, volunteers. Counting is not wrong. For what counting can see, it is honest and useful: it can tell you who showed up, what came in, how many enrolled. A church that refuses to count anything is not more spiritual, only less informed.

But be very clear about what numbers can tell, because here the parable and the whole of Scripture speak with one voice: **everything a church can count, a heart can perform.** Attendance, giving, serving, singing, studying, praying aloud — every observable, countable spiritual behavior can be produced by a transformed heart *or* by a compliant one, and the count comes out identical. The person doing the practices because Christ has captured them and the person doing them because it is expected, habitual, or respectable stand in the same pew and register in the same column. No counter ever built can tell them apart. Neither can the person's own self-report, and this is not cynicism but Scripture's own testimony: "The heart is deceitful above all things, and desperately sick; who can understand it?" (Jeremiah 17:9). We are not reliable witnesses even about ourselves.

The Gospels supply the proof case. The Pharisees scored perfectly on everything countable. Attendance: impeccable. Giving: tithed to the herb garden. Fasting, prayer, Scripture knowledge, public morality: off every chart. Jesus even lets us hear one of them file his self-report: "I fast twice a week; I give tithes of all that I get" (Luke 18:12) — and every word of it was *true*. Yet the Lord's assessment of the whole class was: "you clean the outside of the cup and the plate, but inside they are full of greed and self-indulgence" (Matthew 23:25) — and, more devastating still, that tax collectors and prostitutes were entering the kingdom ahead of them (Matthew 21:31). This is a measurement verdict as much as a moral one: *the best-documented religious performers in Scripture had the worst soil in it.* If countable behavior could reveal the heart, the Pharisees would have been the good ground.

There is a second, stranger limit stacked on the first. Even if everyone answered honestly, the deepest maturity tends to *under*-report itself. The believer in whom Christ's life has become simply who they are does not experience their obedience as remarkable — while the performer, performing partly for their own audience, rates themselves generously. Paul at the end of his life wrote in the present tense: "Christ Jesus came into the world to save sinners, of whom I am the foremost" (1 Timothy 1:15). The most mature are the least likely to claim it.

So what *does* reveal soil? What has always revealed it, and Jesus named the chief of it: "You will recognize them by their fruits" (Matthew 7:16). Which is to say, three slow, unglamorous, deeply pastoral practices no dashboard can replace:

**Patient observation.** Fruit takes seasons, so watching must take seasons. Not a snapshot but a history: how did this congregation handle its last real conflict, its last leadership transition, its last chance to obey at a cost? A wise shepherd watching a church walk through three seasons will learn more about its soil than any survey, because time is the one variable performance cannot fake indefinitely. Rocky ground reveals itself in the heat. Thorns reveal themselves at harvest.

**Honest conversation.** Not questionnaires — conversations. A questionnaire asks what you do; a conversation can gently reach what you love. "Do you read Scripture?" can be answered by a performer. "When you go a week without it, what do you miss?" begins to reach the heart — and answers like that cannot be tallied, only *heard*, by someone the person trusts. A church's capacity to know its own soil is exactly as deep as its relationships are honest.

**Fruit over time.** In the end, the parable's own metric — the only one it offers. Not seed sown (the sower was generous with all four grounds), not green shoots (rocky ground had the fastest), but mature fruit, borne with patience, visible to the people close enough to watch it ripen. And notice: fruit is *observed by others* — Jesus said "you will recognize *them*," not "they will certify themselves."

None of this is as tidy as a spreadsheet, and that is the point. The knowledge a church most needs about itself is pastoral, not statistical — it lives in shepherds who watch, friendships that tell the truth, and years that reveal. Keep the counts for what counting can do. Just never again mistake them for the soil.

## A mirror, not a measuring stick

Now the guardrail — and if you take one paragraph of this chapter into your leadership meeting, let it be this one.

**The four soils are a mirror for self-examination. They are not labels for other people.** The moment this chapter tempts a leadership team to sort its membership — she is thorny ground, he is the hard path, they are our good soil — the chapter has failed, and worse: it has handed the shepherds a tool for the very sin the parable diagnoses. Pinning the labels would be a claim to see hearts, which we have just established we cannot do. It would be judgment rendered on countable appearances — the Pharisees' exact error wearing new vocabulary. And it would freeze as a verdict what Jesus told as an invitation, for the parable ends not with a sorting but with a summons: "He who has ears to hear, let him hear" (Luke 8:8). Jesus told this story to crowds and let every hearer find themselves in it. He did not distribute results.

So there are no scores here, no grades, no ranks of churches, no lists of names — and there never should be. Hold the four soils the way you hold a mirror: *facing yourself.* A leadership team that asks "which soil are *we*? — where is *our* hardness, *our* shallowness, *our* thorns?" is using the parable exactly as given. And when you look, expect the mirror to refuse simple answers, because real ground is patchy: every congregation, like every heart, has some path in it, some rock, some thorns, and — this is the promise worth staking a book on — some ground already good, where the word is held fast and quietly bearing fruit. The purpose of looking is not to award your church a category. It is to find the fallow ground so you can break it up, and the good ground so you can sow toward it.

And soil, remember, is not fate: the whole reason to see your ground truly is that ground can be tilled — Movement III's entire concern.

## The One who sees the heart

One more word — the last, because it stands over everything this movement will attempt.

When Samuel came to Bethlehem to anoint a king and saw Eliab — tall, kingly, obviously the one — the LORD stopped him: "the LORD sees not as man sees: man looks on the outward appearance, but the LORD looks on the heart" (1 Samuel 16:7). If a prophet, on assignment, standing in front of the candidates, could not read hearts by looking — a leadership team with a spreadsheet should hold its conclusions very loosely indeed.

This is the final limit of everything in this chapter, and it is not a defect to be engineered around. It is what it means to stand before a God who alone searches hearts. Every reading we take of our congregation's soil — every patient observation, honest conversation, and season of watched fruit — is provisional, partial, and under correction from the only assessment that is neither. The seven letters showed us a Lord who read seven churches with perfect accuracy, praised what the surveys would have missed, and exposed what the self-reports concealed. He has not resigned that office. We examine our soil *because* He sees it, not instead of Him seeing it — the way a patient examines himself honestly precisely because he is on his way to the Physician. Let the mirror make you humble and honest. It cannot make you the Judge, and it was never meant to.

So: your congregation has soil. It is not the average of its members; it is the culture you share. It cannot be read off a dashboard; it shows itself to patient love over time, and fully only to God. Suppose, then, that you stood in front of this mirror as a leadership team and looked without flinching — at the hard patches, the shallow places, the respectable thorns. One question about your soil would remain that matters more than every classification put together: *are there hungry hearts in it?* Somewhere in your congregation — perhaps in the third pew, perhaps in the quiet couple who never miss and never speak — there may be people already longing for more of God than Sunday is giving them: seed straining toward the light, in ground nobody has thought to ask about. Finding them changes everything about what preparation means. The next chapter is about how to ask.
