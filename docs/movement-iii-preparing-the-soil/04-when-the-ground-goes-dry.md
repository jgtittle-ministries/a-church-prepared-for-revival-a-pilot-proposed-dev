# When the Ground Goes Dry

> **Draft status:** working draft (DEV) — offered for prayer and honest discussion, not yet a program.

Here is a promise this book can make with complete confidence: if your church begins seriously praying and preparing for revival, some of you will go dry.

Not might. Will. The record is unanimous. People who press in toward God — who trade a comfortable Sunday faith for real seeking — pass through seasons when prayer feels like talking to the ceiling, when the Scriptures that once glowed lie flat on the page, when the God they were just beginning to hear seems to have stepped out of the room. And here is the danger: dryness is the most misread experience in the whole spiritual life. Misread it one way, and you crush a soul God is carrying. Misread it the other way, and you comfort a soul God is calling to wake up.

So this chapter exists to prevent one error above all others — the one unforgivable pastoral mistake in a preparing church: **telling a dry believer that their dryness means they are disconnected from God.**

Say that to the wrong person and you have handed the enemy his favorite weapon, sharpened. Because dryness is not one thing. It is at least four things, nearly identical from the outside — same flat prayers, same dull Bible, same absent feelings — while calling for responses so different that two of them are exact opposites. A church that intends to pray its way toward revival must teach its leaders to tell these four apart, because instinct will not do it. Instinct says *feelings gone, God gone.* And instinct, at the exact moment God is doing His deepest work in a person, is usually wrong.

## Four kinds of dry

Walk a farm in a drought and the fields all look the same: brown, cracked, still. But a farmer knows the ground goes dry for different reasons — one field was never watered, one was worked to exhaustion, one has something poisoning the soil, and one has been deliberately left fallow so it can bear more later. Same appearance; four stories; four remedies.

The soul is like that. The tradition of the church — tested across centuries and, remarkably, confirmed by modern research on spiritual struggle — distinguishes four kinds of dryness:

1. **Neglect** — the ground nobody watered. The believer has quietly stopped using the ordinary means of grace: Scripture, prayer, worship, honest fellowship. The dryness is simple thirst, and the remedy is simple drinking.
2. **Over-obligation** — the ground worked to death. The believer has turned faith into a crushing ledger of duties, or serves in a driven, restless overwork that has burned the joy out of everything. The remedy is not more effort; it is rest and a lighter yoke.
3. **The enemy's desolation** — a dry wind sent against the soul. Discouragement with a voice in it, whispering lies, pushing the believer to quit and to reverse good decisions. The remedy is to *resist* — hold course and refuse the counsel.
4. **God's own stripping** — the fallow field; what the old writers called the dark night. God Himself withdraws the *felt* sweetness of His presence, not because He has left, but because He is weaning the soul off the sweetness so it can learn to love Him for Himself. The remedy is to *receive* — wait, stay faithful, and let Him work.

Look hard at the last two, because this is where the stakes live. The enemy's desolation is to be fought. God's stripping is to be welcomed. **Misdiagnose, and you invert the response** — you end up fighting what God is doing, or surrendering to what the enemy is doing. A leader who has only one category — all dryness is backsliding, or all dryness is a holy dark night — will eventually do real harm with the best of intentions.

And one more thing must be said before the four cases, and it belongs in bold: **sometimes the dryness is not spiritual at all.** Depression wears the same gray coat. We will come to that squarely, because getting it wrong there is not a pastoral error but a medical one.

## The one question that sorts them: which way does the desire point?

If you remember nothing else from this chapter, remember this. The four kinds of dryness are not sorted by *how dark it is* — by intensity of feeling, length of the season, or how bad the person says it is. They are sorted by **the direction of the desire underneath.**

Ask the dry believer, gently: *Do you still want Him?* Not "do you feel Him" — they don't; that is the whole problem. Do you still *want* Him? Does the absence ache?

Scripture draws this line in a single verse. Isaiah, speaking to God's own people, says: *"Who among you fears the LORD and obeys the voice of his servant? Let him who walks in darkness and has no light trust in the name of the LORD and rely on his God"* (Isaiah 50:10, ESV). There it is, in one sentence: a person can presently fear the Lord, presently obey — and still be walking in darkness with no light. Darkness is not the diagnosis. And notice what that person is told to do: not repent, not try harder — *trust.* The very next verse turns to a different crowd, the ones who respond to darkness by lighting their own torches, manufacturing their own light — and they are the ones the oracle warns.

Everywhere the Bible speaks of God hiding His face, this line holds. When His hiddenness is judgment, the people's *turning away comes first* and the hiding follows. When His hiddenness falls on the faithful — Job, the psalmists — the desire is still there, intact and starving: *"As a deer pants for flowing streams, so pants my soul for you, O God. My soul thirsts for God, for the living God"* (Psalm 42:1–2, ESV). The psalmist feels abandoned — read the rest of the psalm — but he is *panting.* The ache itself is the health. *"Blessed are those who hunger and thirst for righteousness, for they shall be satisfied"* (Matthew 5:6, ESV). Jesus does not say blessed are the full. The dangerous condition in Scripture is never thirst; it is the church that says *"I need nothing"* (Revelation 3:17, ESV), the people who *"have forsaken me, the fountain of living waters, and hewed out cisterns for themselves"* (Jeremiah 2:13, ESV) — souls that stopped aching and didn't notice.

This is not one tradition's private opinion. John of the Cross taught that in a true dark night the soul remains painfully anxious that it is failing God — his word for the counterfeit was *lukewarmness*, which never worries about such things. The desert fathers found it from the other side: their name for spiritual boredom, *acedia*, is desire turned inside out and given a pious excuse. The Jesuits found it: the persisting desire for God in the middle of felt abandonment is itself evidence of His presence. Mother Teresa's spiritual director found it, and told her so: the sure sign of God's hidden presence in her darkness was precisely her thirst for Him. And the modern psychologists of spiritual struggle have, in their own vocabulary, found the same thing: desire is the diagnostic. Six independent witnesses, one discriminator.

So here is your bedside instrument, and it fits in one sentence: **the drifting soul does not ache.** If the dryness *hurts* — if the person mourns the absence, misses Him, keeps showing up in anguish — you are almost certainly not looking at disconnection. You may be looking at the opposite.

Now let us take the four cases one at a time, the way a Companion or a group leader would actually meet them.

## Case one: the ground nobody watered

**What it is.** The oldest and simplest story: the means of grace quietly abandoned. Prayer thinned out months ago. The Bible sits closed. Worship became optional, then rare. Nobody decided anything; life just crowded in. And one day the person notices — or a friend notices for them — that God feels far away.

**What to ask.** "Tell me about an ordinary week — where does prayer actually live in it? When did you last open the Scriptures just to hear Him?" Not as an audit. As a doctor asking what the patient has been eating.

**What to listen for.** The tell here is *comfort.* The world still tastes fine — work, entertainment, hobbies all still give their pleasure; only God has gone flat. And listen for the absence of ache: the distance doesn't particularly hurt. They may feel vague guilt when asked, but no starved longing. The practice lapsed quietly, and the soul didn't grieve it.

**What to counsel.** Kindly, honestly: this dryness has an ordinary cause and an ordinary cure. *"Draw near to God, and he will draw near to you"* (James 4:8, ESV). No shame, no drama — thirst lifts when you drink. Help them rebuild the simple habits, and put someone alongside them for the first stretch. This is the one case where the dryness genuinely is about distance — and the door back stands open. The kindest thing a leader can do here is *not* to call it a dark night; dressing up neglect in mystical language robs a person of the plain, joyful remedy in front of them.

## Case two: the ground worked to exhaustion

**What it is.** The opposite failure: not too little religion but too much of the wrong kind. The believer has turned grace into a ledger — every missed devotion a debt, every rest a guilt. Or they serve in ceaseless, driven overwork: teaching, hosting, organizing, volunteering, until the machinery of serving God has ground out all delight in God. The old writers knew this restlessness well and noticed its favorite whisper: *I would serve God better somewhere else* — another church, another role, another season. Anywhere but here. It sounds like devotion. It is an exit dressed as devotion.

**What to ask.** "What would happen if you rested — really rested? Who told you God is disappointed in you? When you imagine pleasing Him, do you picture doing *more*?"

**What to listen for.** Restlessness rather than longing. Aversion to the actual duty in front of them, alongside busy fantasies of some other holier life. Faith experienced as weight. Serving continues — often frantically — but joy left long ago, and quiet prayer has become the one thing they cannot bear to sit still for. Where the dark-night soul aches *for* God, this soul mostly wants to escape — and feels weary and irritable about the things of God, which troubles them if they're honest.

**What to counsel.** Not one more obligation. *"Come to me, all who labor and are heavy laden, and I will give you rest. … For my yoke is easy, and my burden is light"* (Matthew 11:28, 30, ESV). The ancient counsel here was almost comically practical: stay put — don't take the "elsewhere" bait; do small faithful work; rest without guilt; pray brief, honest prayers rather than long forced ones. Help the person lay burdens down one at a time and discover that God was never the one loading them. A congregation stirred toward revival will produce over-workers by the dozen — and their burnout will be misread as backsliding unless leaders know this quadrant.

## Case three: the enemy's dry wind

**What it is.** The tradition — and Scripture — insists that some desolation is an attack. Not a mood: a *voice* in the dryness. Discouragement with intent. Its signature is what it counsels: quit the group, drop the commitment, abandon the praying, undo the good decision you made when your head was clear. *"Your adversary the devil prowls around like a roaring lion, seeking someone to devour. Resist him, firm in your faith"* (1 Peter 5:8–9, ESV).

**What to ask.** "What is the dryness *saying* to you? What is it pushing you to do? What decisions are you tempted to reverse right now — decisions you made back when things were clear?"

**What to listen for.** Accusation and urgency. "God has given up on you." "This praying business was a mistake." "Get out now." Listen especially for pressure to *change course* — and for secrecy. This kind of desolation hates the light; the person will often admit they've told no one what's going on inside.

**What to counsel.** Three rules, four and a half centuries old and still unimproved. First: **in a time of desolation, never make a change.** Hold every commitment made in the clear season; the counsel arriving in the darkness is not from God, so don't act on it. Second: change *yourself*, not your decisions — a little more prayer, a little more Scripture, not less; the enemy's aim is to get you to drop your weapons. Third — this is why your church builds Companions before the rain — **tell someone.** The old insight is that this enemy "wants to be secret"; desolation disclosed to a wise friend loses half its power on the spot. And give the sufferer the anchor promise: God permits these sieges but never fully withdraws — grace enough always remains, felt or not, and this kind of desolation *ends.* The instruction is simply to still be standing when it does: *"And let us not grow weary of doing good, for in due season we will reap, if we do not give up"* (Galatians 6:9, ESV).

## Case four: God's own stripping

**What it is.** The deepest case, and the one your instincts will most reliably get wrong. Sometimes God Himself withdraws the felt sweetness — not in anger, and not in absence, but as a mother weans a child: the milk is taken away so the child can learn to eat solid food and walk on its own feet. That is John of the Cross's own image for it. The believer had been loving God partly for the good feelings; God, wanting to be loved for Himself, removes the feelings. Everything goes dark — and the darkness is not His departure. John's boldest claim, and he meant it precisely, is that the dark night *is* "an inflowing of God into the soul": God pressing in so close, so far beyond the soul's current capacity to register, that the excess of light lands as darkness. Presence overwhelming the instruments, not absence.

Scripture states the pattern first, in the wilderness: God *led* His people into the dry place — the leading was His — *"that he might humble you, testing you to know what was in your heart"*; He let them hunger so they could learn a deeper feeding; and the stated purpose, twice: *"to do you good in the end"* (Deuteronomy 8:2, 16, ESV). Even Jesus went from the voice at the Jordan — *beloved Son* — straight into the silence of the wilderness, and Mark says the Spirit *drove* Him there (Mark 1:12). Voice, then silence. It is a pattern, and it is His. *"Every branch … that does bear fruit he prunes, that it may bear more fruit"* (John 15:2, ESV). Notice which branch gets the knife.

**What to ask.** The direction-of-desire questions, softly: "Do you still want Him? Does the absence hurt? Is anything else satisfying you instead — or has the whole world gone flat too? Are you still praying, even though it feels like nothing?"

**What to listen for.** Three signs, together. First: the ache — desire for God intact and starving. Second: *nothing else satisfies either* — unlike the drifter, this person finds the world has gone gray along with the prayer; there is no rival pleasure quietly taking God's place. Third: a painful, persistent worry that they are failing God — the very anxiety is diagnostic, because lukewarm souls do not lose sleep over their lukewarmness. And watch the feet, not the feelings: this person is still showing up. Still praying into the silence. Fidelity in anguish, with nothing to show for it. That is the signature.

**What to counsel.** Here the whole counsel is one word the flesh hates: *wait.* Do not tell them to try harder — effort is not the issue. Do not send them hunting for a technique to get the feelings back; Isaiah's warning about self-kindled torches is aimed exactly at that move. Do not — ever — tell them the darkness means God has left. Tell them the truth: this is well-trodden ground; the saints have a name for it; and every witness who has mapped it says it is the road *into* God, not out of Him. Counsel what Teresa of Ávila counseled: keep doing the works of love, and wait for His mercy — measure the season by faithfulness, not by feelings. Be accompanied; nobody should walk a dark stretch alone. And hold Job's confidence out to them like a lamp: *"But he knows the way that I take; when he has tried me, I shall come out as gold"* (Job 23:10, ESV).

Two honest boundaries, so we do not romanticize. The gentler form of this stripping is common among people who genuinely press in; the *deep* night the mystics describe comes, by the tradition's own account, to very few — it is not something to seek or to prescribe. And God is kinder than the doctrine's reputation: the old writers observed that He gives refreshments along the way, breaks in the clouds, so the soul is not crushed. If you meet someone in total darkness with no relief and collapsing under it, keep reading; the next section is for them.

## When to call the doctor

Everything above assumes the dryness is spiritual. Sometimes it is not — and a preparing church must build this rule into its leaders' bones: **depression is a medical condition, and it wears the same gray coat as every case above.**

Spurgeon — no stranger to either category — put the confusion memorably when he observed that "dyspepsia has been mistaken for backsliding": sometimes the body is the whole story. And the finest modern writer on the dark night, a psychiatrist who spent his career with both conditions, laid down the rule this book adopts without amendment: dark night and depression so often *coexist* that the differential must never be used to withhold clinical care. **Identify and treat depression wherever it exists — dark night or no dark night.** Treating the depression does not interrupt anything God is doing; a medicated saint can still be led through the wilderness. The pruning does not require the person to be unwell.

How does a leader spot the difference? The clinical markers, in plain English: in a dark night, humor survives, compassion for others survives, daily functioning survives, and the person's basic sense of self survives — the darkness is *about God's felt absence*, not about their worthlessness. In depression, everything goes down together: no pleasure in anything, functioning collapsing, humor gone, and above all a settled verdict of self-loathing — *I am worthless, everyone would be better off without me.* The dark night says "God feels absent and I miss Him." Depression says "I am garbage." Those are different sentences.

**Refer to licensed care — promptly and without embarrassment — when you meet any of these:** any talk of suicide or self-harm (this one immediately, that day); voices, delusions, or breaks with reality; someone no longer able to work, care for their family, or get out of bed; a persistent condition that looks diagnosable even if you can't name it; or dryness that shows no movement at all despite months of faithful accompaniment. Referring is not a spiritual failure — not the leader's, and not the sufferer's. The Companion walks with the person; the physician treats the illness; the wisest course is usually both together. Even Jonathan Edwards taught his young friend David Brainerd to distinguish spiritual desertion from the disease of melancholy. That distinction is three centuries old. We have no excuse for losing it.

One quieter condition deserves a sentence, because pastors meet it constantly: some people are neither depressed nor in a dark night — they have simply lost hope and meaning, often after long illness or loss, while still able to enjoy small things. That condition responds to patient, meaning-restoring companionship — presence, story, purpose — more than anything else. Which is exactly what a church full of formed Companions has to give.

## The saints who walked it — and what their darkness produced

If the four-way differential is the chapter's spine, this is its heart, because the record contains a pattern nobody would dare invent.

Mother Teresa of Calcutta experienced roughly six months of extraordinary felt union with God in 1946–47 — the season of her call to the poor. Then, almost exactly as the work began, the light went out. Her private letters, published after her death (she had asked that they be destroyed), record a darkness that lasted, with one brief respite of a few weeks, for around *fifty years* — the entire public ministry. She wrote of the terrible pain of feeling unwanted by God, of doubts she poured out on paper — and notice the detail that gives the story away: she wrote the doubts *as prayers.* Addressed to Him. People who have actually concluded God is gone do not write Him letters saying they miss Him. Through five decades of felt nothing, her practice never broke — up before dawn, daily worship, daily service — and across those same decades the fruit never stopped growing: one house in Calcutta became hundreds of foundations and thousands of sisters across the world. The turning point in her soul came not when the feelings returned — they never did — but when a wise confessor gave her the diagnosis this chapter has been teaching: her very thirst for God was the sure sign of His hidden presence. She wrote afterward that for the first time she had come to love the darkness. The night was never lifted. It was *reinterpreted* — and that was enough to carry a world-changing ministry home.

She is the anchor, not the exception. Charles Spurgeon, perhaps the most fruitful preacher of his century, suffered recurring crushing darkness all his life and left us this sentence: *"This depression comes over me whenever the Lord is preparing a larger blessing for my ministry; the cloud is black before it breaks."* David Brainerd's deepest discouragement came immediately *before* the revival among the native peoples he served — not after failure, before fruit. C. S. Lewis, grieving his wife, described the sense of a door slammed and double-bolted against him — and later understood that the bolt had been felt, not real. Again and again the record places the darkness at the threshold of blessing.

Two honesty notes, because this book promised you honesty. First, the record we have is written by the perseverers — those who quietly walked away did not leave fifty years of letters, so hold the pattern humbly. Second, the modern research is blunt: spiritual struggle is a genuine fork in the road, and *decline, not growth, is the statistical default* for struggle that is suppressed, hidden, and unaccompanied. What routes a soul toward the growth branch is exactly what this movement has been building all along: honest engagement instead of suppression, meaning found inside the struggle, and — above everything — companions to whom the darkness can be *spoken.* A church where dryness is unspeakable is a church manufacturing quiet collapses. A church where dryness can be said out loud, met with this differential and without a verdict, is a church whose dry seasons become root systems.

## The card in the Companion's pocket

Here is the whole differential, small enough to copy out. The sorting question is always the same: *which way does the desire point?*

| | **1. Neglect** | **2. Over-obligation** | **3. Enemy's desolation** | **4. God's stripping (dark night)** |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Desire for God** | Faded; distance doesn't ache | Buried under duty; wants escape, not God | Under assault; accused and pressured | Intact and starving — the ache is the sign |
| **Rest of life** | World still tastes fine | Restless churn; "I'd serve better elsewhere" | Urgency, fear, secrecy | World has gone gray too; no rival pleasure |
| **Practice** | Quietly lapsed, ungrieved | Frantic or ledger-driven, joyless | Tempted to quit and reverse good decisions | Persists in anguish, feels like nothing |
| **The tell** | No ache at all | The "anywhere but here" whisper | A voice counseling change: *quit, undo, leave* | Painful worry about failing God (lukewarm souls never worry) |
| **First counsel** | Wake and turn; rebuild the means of grace (James 4:8) | Rest; lighter yoke; stay put; smaller faithful steps (Matt 11:28–30) | **Resist**: change nothing, pray a little more, tell someone (1 Pet 5:8–9) | **Receive**: wait, stay faithful, be accompanied; works of love + patience (Job 23:10) |

**Always, in every case:** if you see talk of self-harm, loss of basic functioning, self-loathing verdicts, or global collapse of pleasure and humor and compassion — refer to licensed care, and keep walking alongside while the professionals work. The two callings cooperate; they do not compete.

And notice, one last time, that cases 3 and 4 command opposite motions — *fight* versus *yield* — which is the entire reason this card must be taught before the rain and not improvised at the bedside.

## If you are reading this in the dry place

Maybe you did not come to this chapter as a leader. Maybe you came as the dry one — you started praying along with your church, something real began, and then the lights went out, and someone (perhaps only the voice in your own head) has been telling you it means God looked at you and left.

Then hear this plainly. **The fact that His absence hurts you is the evidence that He has not gone.** Drifting hearts do not ache. Abandoned fields do not thirst. *"My soul thirsts for you; my flesh faints for you, as in a dry and weary land where there is no water"* (Psalm 63:1, ESV) — that is not the prayer of a person God has discarded; the Bible holds it up as the prayer of a person God is drawing. The darkest psalm in the whole Psalter ends without a ray of light — *"my companions have become darkness"* (Psalm 88:18, ESV) — and God put it in His songbook, assigned to a wise man, so that you would have words for the worst mile and know that praying them is still praying. And when the Son of God hung in the deepest darkness a soul has ever entered, He prayed, *"My God, my God, why have you forsaken me?"* (Matthew 27:46, ESV) — which settles one question forever: felt absence cannot, by itself, be evidence of sin. He was sinless, and He felt it too. He knows this road from the inside.

So do not grade yourself by your feelings; God never did. Keep showing up — badly, dryly, five honest minutes at a time. Tell one trustworthy person; darkness spoken aloud loses half its weight. Make no big decisions until the light returns. See a doctor without shame if the gray has swallowed everything and you have begun to despise yourself — that is a treatable illness, not a spiritual verdict, and getting help is obedience. And then wait, in the company of Teresa and Spurgeon and the psalmists and a great cloud of the dry-but-faithful, for the God who tests His people *"to do you good in the end"* (Deuteronomy 8:16, ESV). The psalmist in the drought talked to his own soul, and you may borrow his words: *"Why are you cast down, O my soul, and why are you in turmoil within me? Hope in God; for I shall again praise him, my salvation and my God"* (Psalm 42:11, ESV).

The ground goes dry in every farm worth planting. The farmer has not left the field.
