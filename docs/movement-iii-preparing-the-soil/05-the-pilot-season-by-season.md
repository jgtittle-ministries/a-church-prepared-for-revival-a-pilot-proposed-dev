# The Pilot, Season by Season

> **Draft status:** working draft (DEV) — offered for prayer and honest discussion.

Everything in this book so far has been conviction and diagnosis. Movement I asked whether you want a revival at all. Movement II asked what your soil honestly is, and how to find the hungry hearts in it without harming them. This movement has walked the preparation itself — the leadership first, the vessel before the rain, the practices that till, the dry seasons rightly read. Now comes the part a proposal owes you: what a willing church would actually *run*.

What follows is that proposal, laid out season by season. Read it with two rules in hand, because they govern everything on this page.

**Seasons, not dates.** Nothing here has a calendar attached. A season is done when its work is done, and its work is done in hearts, which do not keep schedules. One church may walk Season One in four months; another may need a year and be the healthier for taking it. The pilot has an order — the order is most of the message — but it has no clock. The farmer in James waits for the precious fruit of the earth, *"being patient about it, until it receives the early and the late rains"* (James 5:7, ESV). Patience is not the pilot's weakness. It is one of its load-bearing walls.

**Gates, not deadlines.** Each season ends not with a date but with a question — asked out loud, answered honestly, in prayer. And at every gate, *"not yet"* is a fully honorable answer. Not a failure, not a verdict: an honest reading of the ground, and often the most obedient sentence in the room. A church that says "not yet" at a gate and keeps working the season it is in has lost nothing. A church that pushes through a gate it has not honestly passed has planted its later failure at the exact spot where it skipped the question.

One more word before the seasons: adapt freely. This is a proposal, not a franchise. Your church's size, polity, history, and wounds are yours; the shape below is offered for testing, and it expects your hands on it. What should survive every adaptation is the order, the gates, and the guardrails. Everything else is yours to fit.

## Season Zero — The Decision

**Purpose.** To answer this book's title question honestly, as a leadership, before anyone announces anything — because a revival prayed for casually is a tower begun without an estimate.

**What actually happens.** The leadership — pastors, elders, and whoever genuinely carries the spiritual weight of the church, whatever your polity calls them — agrees to read the first two movements of this book together. One movement per meeting: an evening on Movement I, an evening on Movement II. Read beforehand, gather to talk honestly, and pray — actually pray, not open-and-close pray. No congregational announcement. No vision series. No committee. Just the people responsible for the flock, sitting with the question the book asks and refusing to answer it rhetorically.

The conversation to have is the one Jesus prescribed for exactly this moment: *"For which of you, desiring to build a tower, does not first sit down and count the cost, whether he has enough to complete it? Otherwise, when he has laid a foundation and is not able to finish, all who see it begin to mock him, saying, 'This man began to build and was not able to finish'"* (Luke 14:28–30, ESV). Count the cost, out loud, with the record open. Revival, if God sends it, will rearrange this church — its calendar, its comfort, its leadership's private lives first of all. Movement I laid out what the fire has actually cost the churches that received it. Movement II laid out what an honest look at your own soil will require, and the guardrails that looking demands. The question on the table is not "do we like this book." It is: *do we want the thing itself?*

Expect this season to be quieter and harder than it sounds. Some leaderships will discover, to their joy, a hunger they had never said out loud to each other. Some will discover honest hesitation — a member of the circle who says, "I don't think I want this, and I need to say so." Honor that voice. It has just proven the room can hold truth, which is worth more to this pilot than unanimous enthusiasm, and it is asking the question everyone else should be asking too.

**The gate question.** *Do we want this enough to go first?* Not enough to sponsor it, schedule it, or delegate it — enough to *go first*: to have our own soil tilled before we ask anyone else, to be the first hearts opened and the first calendars rearranged. If the honest answer is "not yet," stop here, in peace, and keep the question alive in prayer. Nothing has been lost, and something important has been learned. If the answer is yes — say so to one another, in plain words, and step through the gate.

**The chapter that equips it.** [The Question](reader.html#docs/movement-i-do-you-want-a-revival/01-the-question.md) and the whole of Movements I and II are this season's curriculum — with [The Guardrails](reader.html#docs/movement-ii-what-is-your-soil/03-the-guardrails.md) read twice.

## Season One — The Leadership Becomes a Fellowship of the Heart

**Purpose.** To make the leadership's own circle the first plot of tilled ground — because a congregation will not go where its leadership has not gone, and there is no route around this season. It is the pilot's foundation, and it is where the pilot most often will be tempted to cheat.

**What actually happens.** The leadership covenants together for a season — months, not weeks; think in terms of two seasons of the year, not two sermon series — to become, themselves, a small fellowship of the heart. Not to *study* the practices. To live under them, together:

- **The practices, weekly.** Scripture engaged as address, expecting the living God to speak; listening prayer, learned as a practice by people willing to be beginners at it; honest fellowship — story told, confession made, truth spoken in love. The circle walks the Four Connects in order in its own room: honest self-knowledge first, real knowing of one another second, hearing God third, and only then talk of mission. [The Practices That Till](reader.html#docs/movement-iii-preparing-the-soil/03-the-practices-that-till.md) is this season's working manual, and [It Starts at the Top](reader.html#docs/movement-iii-preparing-the-soil/01-it-starts-at-the-top.md) is its charter.
- **The guardrails, adopted in writing.** During this season the leadership takes the seven commitments of [The Guardrails](reader.html#docs/movement-ii-what-is-your-soil/03-the-guardrails.md) — mirrors not levers, no scores, consent and daylight, the least-powerful test, the bad-news test, wounds are not weeds, the shepherd's scale — and adopts them formally, in writing, as *this church's own commitments*, before a single congregational conversation happens. Unwritten limits do not hold. Write them while they cost nothing, so they will hold when they cost something.
- **The bad-news test, run on yourselves.** Somewhere in this season, ask the room: *what happened to the last person who told us something we did not want to hear?* Name the person. Sit with the answer. If it is painful, that is not a reason to abandon the pilot — it is the first fallow ground the pilot has located, and it is yours, and the season exists to plow it.

Expect dryness in this season; a circle that begins genuinely praying will meet it, and it will hit the leadership first. Read [When the Ground Goes Dry](reader.html#docs/movement-iii-preparing-the-soil/04-when-the-ground-goes-dry.md) *before* it arrives, so that the first elder whose prayer goes flat is met with the differential and not with a verdict.

This season cannot be performed, and its length cannot be negotiated down, because what it produces cannot be faked: leaders who are *"examples to the flock"* (1 Peter 5:3, ESV) — not experts on the material but people visibly, currently on the journey. The people will read your interior lives faster than they read any announcement.

**The gate question.** *Is our circle real yet — would the bad-news test pass in our room?* Concretely: has each of us been honestly known here? Has hard truth been spoken upward in this circle and honored? Have we heard God together — not talked about hearing Him, heard Him — and obeyed something He said? If the honest answer is "not yet," stay in the season. Nothing built on an unreal leadership circle will be real anywhere else.

**The chapter that equips it.** [It Starts at the Top](reader.html#docs/movement-iii-preparing-the-soil/01-it-starts-at-the-top.md), with [The Practices That Till](reader.html#docs/movement-iii-preparing-the-soil/03-the-practices-that-till.md) as the manual and [The Guardrails](reader.html#docs/movement-ii-what-is-your-soil/03-the-guardrails.md) as the covenant.

## Season Two — Finding the Hungry Hearts

**Purpose.** To discover whom God has already made hungry — not to create hunger, which no church can do, but to find the hearts He has prepared, one conversation at a time.

**What actually happens.** The leaders formed in Season One — and only they, at first, because these conversations should be had by people who have themselves been asked the questions and answered them honestly — begin having the Four Connects conversations described in [Hungry Hearts](reader.html#docs/movement-ii-what-is-your-soil/02-hungry-hearts.md). Gently. With consent, never as an ambush. Over coffee, after the service, on a walk: *How connected do you feel, really, to your own heart? Is there anyone who knows what's actually going on inside you? When you read Scripture or pray, does God ever feel present? Do you have a sense of what He's made you for?* Four questions, fifteen minutes, no clipboard.

And the rules of the road, absolute because the guardrails were adopted in writing last season: no forms, no scores, no reports. What a leader learns is held *for* the person — in prayer, in follow-up, in confidence — never over them. Anything that travels to the leadership travels stripped of names: *many among us are hungrier than Sunday is feeding*, never a list of who. Every conversation ends with something in hand — a first step the person can take this week, because the Four Connects are made of the path itself — and, where real hunger surfaces, the one sentence this whole book keeps arriving at: *would you want company on it?*

Let this season breathe. Dozens of conversations across months, not a blitz across weeks. And let the leaders bring what they are finding back to their own circle — as prayer, not as data — because what they find will move them, and it should. Most churches that walk this season report the same astonishment: the soil held far more hunger than anyone guessed, third row on the left, running the sound board, teaching the four-year-olds.

**The gate question.** *Whom has God already made hungry — and are we ready to feed them?* Both halves matter. If the conversations have surfaced hungry hearts, you are holding a trust, and the only thing worse than never finding your hungry hearts is finding them and failing to feed them. So before the invitation goes out: are the companions ready? Is the table actually built? If the hunger is real but the vessel is not ready, the honest answer is "not yet" — and the next season's work is urgent.

**The chapter that equips it.** [Hungry Hearts](reader.html#docs/movement-ii-what-is-your-soil/02-hungry-hearts.md), held under [The Guardrails](reader.html#docs/movement-ii-what-is-your-soil/03-the-guardrails.md).

## Season Three — The Vessel

**Purpose.** To open the first fellowship groups — the tables where the hunger that Season Two surfaced can actually sit down and be fed — and, inside them, to begin forming the second bench, so that the vessel grows with what God pours into it.

**What actually happens.** The first small fellowship groups open, by invitation, for the hungry hearts already found. At the front of each group stand people formed in Season One — not the most polished teachers, but the most honestly formed hearts; people currently living the practices, not people who once read about them. The groups walk the Four Connects in order, in circles small enough for every face to be known — think eight, not eighteen — under the same container of safety, presence, and consent the leadership learned in its own season, and under the same guardrails, which the group's members should hear stated out loud in the first meeting. Working mechanics for all of this exist and have been piloted — group rhythms, companion preparation, the practices session by session — in [*Fellowship of the Heart*](https://jgtittle-ministries.github.io/fellowship-of-the-heart-pilot-at-cca/), which a church can adapt rather than invent; this book deliberately does not reproduce what that handbook already carries. Two notes from its experience are worth carrying even here: companions lead in pairs, never alone; and a companion's own preparation — weeks of personal work *before* the group's first meeting — is not optional, because you cannot lead someone where you have not been.

And from the first week, the groups do one more thing on purpose: they watch for the next leaders. Inside every real fellowship group, God tends to surface one or two hearts with a shepherd's instincts — the member who notices who is missing, who follows up unasked, who holds the room's trust. Name them early (to yourselves, not to the room), and begin forming them deliberately: give them small pieces of the group to carry, bring them alongside the companions, let them apprentice in the work while the work is happening. This is Paul's four-generation pattern in a single verse: *"what you have heard from me in the presence of many witnesses entrust to faithful men, who will be able to teach others also"* (2 Timothy 2:2, ESV). The second bench is not a nice-to-have. It is the whole point of building the vessel before the rain — because if God sends more than you prepared for, the difference between a revival received and a revival spilled will be measured in formed companions. *"The harvest is plentiful, but the laborers are few; therefore pray earnestly to the Lord of the harvest to send out laborers into his harvest"* (Matthew 9:37–38, ESV). Pray that prayer — and be the church in which the laborers He sends can be formed.

**The gate question.** *Could we open another room — and who would be at its front?* If you cannot name the people who would lead the next group, the vessel has stopped growing, and the season's remaining work is bench-building, not expansion. If you can name them — because they have been forming inside the groups all along — open the room, and ask the question again in a year. This gate, unlike the others, never fully closes; a church in this pilot is always either opening a room or forming the people who will.

**The chapter that equips it.** [The Vessel Before the Rain](reader.html#docs/movement-iii-preparing-the-soil/02-the-vessel-before-the-rain.md).

## Ongoing — Keeping

**Purpose.** To make preparation permanent — because the drift downward never retires, and the churches that kept what came were the ones that had built for keeping before the rain fell.

**What actually happens.** Four standing disciplines, none of them dramatic, all of them the difference between a church that received something and a church that kept it:

- **The practices become congregational rhythm.** Hearing God in Scripture, listening prayer, honest fellowship — not a season the church once ran, but the way this church now lives: in its groups, its leadership meetings, its households. The moment the practices become memorial rather than meal, the drift has begun, whatever the attendance says.
- **The dryness differential is taught to every group leader.** Every person who will ever sit across from a dry believer learns the four kinds of dryness and the one sorting question — *which way does the desire point?* — before they need it. A praying church will go through dry seasons; the one unforgivable pastoral mistake is telling a dry believer their dryness means God has left. [When the Ground Goes Dry](reader.html#docs/movement-iii-preparing-the-soil/04-when-the-ground-goes-dry.md) is standing curriculum for every leader, permanently.
- **The failure modes are reviewed yearly.** Once a year, the leadership sits down with [How Revivals Were Lost](reader.html#docs/movement-iv-keeping-what-comes/02-how-revivals-were-lost.md) open and asks, mode by mode: is the form outliving the power anywhere among us? Are thorns growing from our own harvest? Is the fire quietly housing itself in one person? Are we awakening people we are not forming? Is any quarrel eating the flame? Are we misreading a dry season as failure? An hour of honest answers, annually, is the cheapest insurance in the whole record.
- **Renewal is built in.** Structures are examined on a schedule, not at a crisis: every ministry, group, and rhythm the pilot created gets asked, regularly, whether the wine is still in it — and retired honorably when it is not. The wineskin stays supple only if someone is charged with keeping it supple; [The Drift Downward](reader.html#docs/movement-iv-keeping-what-comes/01-the-drift-downward.md) carries the discipline. Nothing this pilot builds is sacred. Only what it was built to hold is.

**The gate question.** Asked at the yearly review, every year, without exception: *are we still receiving — or only running?* The day the honest answer is "only running," the church has not failed; it has been told exactly where the plow goes next. That is what the gate is for.

**The chapters that equip it.** [When the Ground Goes Dry](reader.html#docs/movement-iii-preparing-the-soil/04-when-the-ground-goes-dry.md), [The Drift Downward](reader.html#docs/movement-iv-keeping-what-comes/01-the-drift-downward.md), and [How Revivals Were Lost](reader.html#docs/movement-iv-keeping-what-comes/02-how-revivals-were-lost.md) — Movement IV is this season's permanent bookshelf.

## What this pilot is not

Three honest sentences before the close, so that no one takes from this chapter more than it offers.

**It is not a revival guarantee.** Nothing on this page obligates God, and nothing ever has. The kingdom, Jesus said, is *"as if a man should scatter seed on the ground. He sleeps and rises night and day, and the seed sprouts and grows; he knows not how"* (Mark 4:26–27, ESV). The pilot prepares soil; it does not schedule rain. A church might walk every season faithfully and see, in its own generation, only quiet deepening — hungrier prayer, realer fellowship, a bench of formed hearts — and that church has not failed. It has become good ground, which is commanded whether or not the extraordinary comes on our schedule, and it has handed the next generation a field ready for whatever God sends. He is not managed. He is sought.

**It is not a program to bolt on.** If this proposal enters a church as one more initiative — announced from the platform, assigned to a staff member, run alongside everything else unchanged — it will fail, and it should. What is proposed here is a culture, and cultures are adopted top-first or not at all: the leadership goes first, lives it first, is changed by it first, and only then invites. A church that wants the seasons without Season One is asking for the harvest without the plowing, and the record of that request is unbroken in one direction.

**It is not finished.** This book is a proposal, offered for testing — and a proposal that expects to be corrected. The guardrails commit us to honoring anyone who says *this process is hurting people* or *this isn't working*, and that commitment covers the process itself. Churches that take up this pilot will find things this book got wrong, seasons that need reordering for their soil, gates that need sharpening. Send that news back. It is not criticism of the proposal; it is participation in it, and future drafts expect to be different because of it.

## Prepare, test, get started — and keep

Here, then, is the whole arc of this book, gathered in one place. **Prepare:** the question answered honestly, the soil examined, the leadership tilled first. **Test:** the gates, season by season, with "not yet" always allowed — because an honest gate failed is worth more than a dishonest gate passed. **Get started:** the conversations, the tables, the first rooms opened for the hungry hearts God prepared before you went looking. **And prepare to keep what comes:** the differential taught, the failure modes reviewed, the wineskin kept supple — the barn mended before the harvest, because the record was written, every time, by the preparation.

None of it is exotic. Every season on this page is made of things an ordinary church can do in ordinary time: read together, pray together, tell the truth, adopt commitments, have conversations, open small rooms, form the next bench, and keep asking honest questions on a schedule. That is the proposal. That has always been the proposal — it is older than this book by some twenty-seven centuries: *"Sow for yourselves righteousness; reap steadfast love; break up your fallow ground, for it is the time to seek the LORD, that he may come and rain righteousness upon you"* (Hosea 10:12, ESV).

The seasons are before you. The gates are honest. The ground is yours.

It is the time to seek the LORD.
