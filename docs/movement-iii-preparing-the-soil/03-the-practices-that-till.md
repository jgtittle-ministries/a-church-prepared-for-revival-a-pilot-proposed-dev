# The Practices That Till

> **Draft status:** working draft (DEV) — offered for prayer and honest discussion.

Somewhere in the last two chapters, a question has probably been forming in you, and it is the right question: *all right — but what do we actually do?* The leadership has begun its own tilling. The hungry hearts are being found, one unhurried conversation at a time, and each of them is being offered a path built on the Four Connects — with self, with others, with God, with mission ([Hungry Hearts](reader.html#docs/movement-ii-what-is-your-soil/02-hungry-hearts.md) laid out that whole map). Now comes the practical center of this movement: the practices themselves. The ordinary, repeatable things a person — and a fellowship, and eventually a congregation — actually does, week by week, so that hard ground softens.

Before naming them, one thing must be settled, because everything else in this chapter hangs on it.

## One seed, many tillings

There is one seed in this book, and only one: **hearing and obeying God.** Not hearing *about* Him — hearing Him, above all in Scripture, and doing what He says. That is the life the ordinary believer is invited into; it is what every hungry heart in your pews is actually hungry for, whether or not they have the words; and it is what every revival in the record was, at its core — a great many people beginning to hear and obey God at once.

Everything in this chapter serves that one seed. The practices below are not five separate spiritual projects, five boxes to check, five more things for busy people to feel guilty about. They are five ways of tending the same ground so that one seed can live in it. A farmer does many different things in a field — plowing, watering, weeding, waiting — but he is not raising five crops. He is raising one, and every motion of his hands serves it. Hold the practices that way. When any of them becomes an end in itself — when a person starts being *good at devotions* rather than coming to hear God — the practice has quietly stopped tilling and started decorating.

## What the practices cannot do — and why that is good news

Now the boundary, and it must be stated plainly, because more spiritual damage has been done by forgetting it than by almost anything else a sincere church can do.

**The practices do not produce life. They tend conditions. God gives the growth.**

The longer work behind this book, which studied these practices in depth, states the boundary in one sentence: formation tends conditions; the Spirit gives the growth. That is not a disclaimer tacked on for humility's sake. It is the load-bearing wall of the whole chapter, and Scripture sets it exactly: *"So neither he who plants nor he who waters is anything, but only God who gives the growth"* (1 Corinthians 3:7, ESV). Paul had planted; Apollos had watered; the labor was real and necessary — and the decisive act belonged to neither of them. Jesus made the same point in a parable that ought to hang over every discipleship program ever devised: a man scatters seed, sleeps and rises night and day, *"and the seed sprouts and grows; he knows not how"* (Mark 4:27, ESV). The farmer's work fills the parable. The growth is not his.

Why is this good news rather than discouragement? Two reasons.

First, it takes the crushing weight off. If the practices produced life, then a season when they feel lifeless would mean you are failing, and a person who prays would be manufacturing God's presence like a product. Neither is true. Your part is to show up in the field — to read, to listen, to obey, to gather, to serve. The germination was never your job. You can stop straining at what was never asked of you.

Second, it protects the church from its oldest counterfeit. A congregation that forgets this boundary will eventually start measuring devotional output and calling it maturity — mistaking the watering can for the harvest. The person who *does all the practices* and the person whose heart is being transformed can look identical from the outside; only the boundary keeps a church honest about the difference. We till. He grows. Everything below stands under that sentence.

With the boundary set, here are the practices — each in plain words, each with a first step a real person could take this week.

## Unhurried Scripture: reading to hear, not to finish

The first practice is Scripture — but a particular way into it, and the way matters as much as the book.

Most Bible reading in most churches is reading to *finish*: a chapter a day, a plan to complete, ground to cover. Nothing wrong with coverage; the whole Bible is worth knowing. But reading-to-finish trains a habit of motion, and hearing requires a habit of stillness. The practice this book proposes is older and slower: reading to *hear*. You come to a short passage — a paragraph, even a verse — not as information to process but as a word addressed to you, because *"faith comes from hearing, and hearing through the word of Christ"* (Romans 10:17, ESV). You read it slowly. You read it again. You linger where something snags — a phrase that glows, a sentence that stings — because the snag is often where God is speaking. You ask Him about it. You sit with it. You let it read *you*.

The church has practiced this for many centuries under various names; you do not need any of them. You need ten minutes, a short passage, and one change of posture: you are not trying to get through the text. You are trying to let God get through to you. The psalmist tasted Scripture this way — *sweeter than honey to my mouth* (Psalm 119:103, paraphrase) — and honey is not eaten by the chapter.

**A first step this week:** take one short passage — the twenty-third Psalm will do, or a paragraph from John 15 — and give it ten unhurried minutes. Read it three times, slower each time. When a word or phrase catches you, stop there. Ask, *"Lord, what are You saying to me in this?"* — and do not rush the silence that follows. One passage, ten minutes, three mornings this week. That is the whole assignment.

## Listening prayer: speaking less, waiting more

The second practice is prayer — again, a particular posture of it.

Most of us learned prayer as talking: we bring the list, we say the words, we close in Jesus' name. Talking to God is right and commanded. But a conversation in which one party only talks is not yet a relationship, and the God of Scripture speaks — to Samuel, to Elijah, to the church at Antioch, to *"My sheep"* who *"hear My voice"* (John 10:27, paraphrase). Listening prayer is simply prayer with the proportions changed: speaking less, waiting more. You bring God a real question — not a test, a genuine one: *"Father, what do You want me to see about this?"* — and then, instead of immediately supplying the answer yourself, you wait. You attend. You notice what rises: a verse of Scripture surfacing, a quiet impression, a name, a settled conviction, sometimes nothing at all. The boy Samuel's prayer is the whole posture in six words: *"Speak, LORD, for your servant hears"* (1 Samuel 3:9, ESV).

And here the guardrail, as load-bearing as the practice: **everything heard is tested against Scripture, and Scripture always wins.** God does not contradict His own Word. An impression that runs against the Bible is not from Him, however vivid it felt; an impression that would harm, isolate, exalt, or excuse you should be held with great suspicion; and anything weighty gets tested a second way — with mature believers, in the fellowship, over time. No private revelation ever stands over the written Word. This discipline is not a limit on hearing God. It is what makes hearing Him *safe* — the difference between a listening people and a gullible one. The mystics knew it; the Reformers insisted on it; this book requires it.

**A first step this week:** each morning before you pick up your phone, sit for five minutes with one question — *"Father, what are You up to today, and what do You want me to notice?"* In the evening, write one sentence about what you noticed. That practice comes straight from the first week of the pilot fellowship this book leans on, and a twelve-year-old can do it. So can you.

## Obedience in small things: the amplifier

The third practice is the one that makes the first two grow — and the one most books on spiritual disciplines quietly under-weight.

Here is the dynamic, and it may be the single most practical sentence in this chapter: **hearing grows by being obeyed.** Jesus stated it as a plain condition: *"If anyone's will is to do God's will, he will know whether the teaching is from God"* (John 7:17, ESV). Notice the order. The willingness to obey comes first; the knowing follows. A heart that listens with no intention of acting will find the voice growing fainter — not because God stopped speaking but because He does not entrust more to those who shelved what He already said. And a heart that acts on the little it hears — promptly, even clumsily — finds the hearing getting clearer, the way a muscle strengthens by being used. Obedience is the amplifier. *"Blessed rather are those who hear the word of God and keep it!"* (Luke 11:28, ESV).

This is why the practice is obedience *in small things.* Do not wait for God to assign you a continent. The word that comes in unhurried Scripture and listening prayer is usually small and specific: apologize to her. Call your brother. Give that away. Stop saying that. Sit with the lonely one on Sunday. Small obediences are not the minor leagues of the spiritual life; they are where the entire capacity to hear and obey is actually built — and Scripture warns us not to despise *the day of small things* (Zechariah 4:10, paraphrase). The person faithful in very little is the one who can be trusted with much; Jesus said so, and revivals are made of people who became trustworthy in very little first.

**A first step this week:** when something you read or hear in prayer carries a small, concrete instruction — and one will — do it within forty-eight hours. Do not evaluate it to death; if it is scriptural, harmless, and humble, act. Then notice, honestly, what happens to your hearing.

## Honest fellowship: the practices done together

The fourth practice changes the room. Everything so far could be done alone; this one, by definition, cannot — and it is the one that turns a handful of private devotional lives into soil a revival could actually take root in.

Honest fellowship means the practices above are done *together* — in a small circle where truth is told. Not a class where content is covered. Not a social hour with a prayer stapled on. A circle — the pilot fellowship behind this book uses circles of eight to twelve — where people read Scripture listening together and say what they heard; where they pray and wait together; where impressions are tested in the open instead of nursed in private; where confession is normal, because *"confess your sins to one another and pray for one another, that you may be healed"* (James 5:16, ESV) was written to ordinary congregations, not monasteries; where encouragement is specific and honest speech is safe. Jesus attached a promise to exactly this scale: *"For where two or three are gathered in my name, there am I among them"* (Matthew 18:20, ESV).

Why is this a *tilling* practice and not merely a nice addition? Three reasons. Hearing is checked here — the circle is where listening prayer gets its testing ground, and a leading that survives the loving scrutiny of honest companions is worth far more than one that never faced it. Hunger is contagious here — desire for God is caught from people who have it more reliably than it is taught from any lectern. And hiddenness dies here — the hard, packed crust on most church soil is not unbelief but concealment, dozens of people surrounded weekly and known by nobody, and no plow reaches that crust except being truthfully known. One warning from long experience: the honesty must be *invited, never extracted*. A circle that demands vulnerability produces performance, which is just concealment wearing a warmer face. Go first, go gently, and let trust be caught rather than commanded.

**A first step this week:** you do not need a program to begin. Ask one or two people — the hungry hearts you already know about are the obvious ones — *"Would you want to meet for an hour a week to read Scripture slowly, pray, and actually tell each other the truth?"* Set a time. That sentence, asked in earnest, has started more of what this book hopes for than any curriculum ever printed.

## Simple service: hearing completed in going

The fifth practice keeps the whole enterprise from curving inward — and it is where the fourth Connect, mission, becomes a practice rather than an aspiration.

A circle that reads, listens, obeys, and confesses — and never leaves the room — will slowly turn its formation into a hobby. The seed in this book is hearing *and obeying* God, and much of what God says, when people genuinely listen, points outward: toward a neighbor, a need, a task He prepared in advance. *"For we are his workmanship, created in Christ Jesus for good works, which God prepared beforehand, that we should walk in them"* (Ephesians 2:10, ESV). The works already exist; service is how we walk into them. This is hearing completed in going — the listening life proving itself in love, without which, Scripture warns, all our words are a noisy gong.

Keep the practice *simple*, and keep it downstream of listening. Not a new committee; not a program requiring a budget line. The practice is this: the circle (or the person) asks God, in listening prayer, *"Who have You put in front of us, and what would love look like this month?"* — tests what comes, and then does one concrete thing. A meal cooked. A widow's gutters cleaned. A single mother's Saturday relieved. An hour given to the kid nobody else has time for. Small, local, and actually done. Service of this kind waters everything else: obedience gets exercised, the circle's hearing gets confirmed or corrected by reality, and the congregation around you starts to see what a listening people looks like — which, quietly, is the best invitation a hungry heart ever receives.

**A first step this week:** ask the question above — alone or with your circle — wait, and write down the first name or need that comes. Test it against Scripture and common sense. If it stands, do one thing about it before the week is out.

## Start small, and start where people actually are

Five practices. Now two pieces of gardener's wisdom about *how* to take them up, both learned the hard way by generations of earnest churches, and both documented at length in the study behind this chapter.

The first: **match the practice to the person, and do not hand a novice a monastery.** The full practices paper behind this book makes the case in careful detail; here is its plain-English core. A person just waking up to spiritual hunger does not need the regimen of a mature contemplative — they need one small structure: five minutes of morning listening and one honest companion. A person with years of dutiful Bible reading behind them and no life in it does not need *more discipline* — pressure is precisely the wrong tool there; they need the unhurried, delight-first reading that lets them taste again what they have long since stopped tasting. A person already hearing God in Scripture may be ready for the harder gardening — letting what they hear reorder their calendar, their money, their ambitions. The same practice that is exactly right for one person is exactly wrong for their neighbor, and much sincere discipleship fails not because its practices are bad but because they are handed out one-size-fits-all. Leaders: this is why the unhurried conversations of Movement II matter so much. You cannot match the practice to the person if you have never asked where the person actually is.

The second: **rhythm beats intensity — every time, in every recorded case.** The temptation, especially in a church stirred by talk of revival, is the heroic start: the two-hour devotional plan, the 5 a.m. covenant, the twenty-one-day everything. Heroic starts have a documented life expectancy of about three weeks, and they leave discouragement behind them — soil harder than it was before, because now it has *failed at revival* too. Small and sustained beats heroic and abandoned, always. Ten minutes every morning for a year will do more tilling than a monthly all-nighter, for the same reason that steady rain grows crops and a flash flood strips topsoil. Fruit, Jesus said, is borne *with patience* (Luke 8:15, paraphrase) — and patience is a rhythm word. Set the bar where an ordinary tired adult can clear it in an ordinary tired week, and then clear it, week after week, together. *"And let us not grow weary of doing good, for in due season we will reap, if we do not give up"* (Galatians 6:9, ESV).

## When it goes dry — and it will

One more thing, so that nobody is ambushed.

If your people take up these practices in earnest, then at some point — three months in, or three years — many of them will hit a season when it all goes flat. The Scripture that tasted like honey tastes like paper. The listening prayer returns silence. The circle feels routine. And the person, having been told these practices would open their life to God, quietly concludes that something is broken — in the practices, or worse, in themselves — and quits at the exact moment they should not.

Hear this now, before it happens: **dryness is expected, it is survivable, and it is very often not what it appears to be.** The deepest voices in the church's long history agree that God Himself sometimes withdraws the *feeling* of His presence from people He is taking deeper — precisely so that their obedience grows roots below the level of feeling. Not every dryness is that; some dryness is drift, and some is exhaustion, and the differences matter enormously — which is why the next chapter, [When the Ground Goes Dry](reader.html#docs/movement-iii-preparing-the-soil/04-when-the-ground-goes-dry.md), gives leaders the full differential. For now, plant this single flag where everyone can see it: when the practices go dry, the standing instruction is *do not panic, and do not quit.* Keep the rhythm. Tell your circle. Stay in the field. Some of the hungriest, most alive hearts in your congregation are dry right now — and the farmer who leaves the field in a dry spell forfeits the harvest that was already on its way.

## When a congregation tills together

Step back now and see the whole picture, because the practices change scale.

One person reading unhurriedly, listening, obeying small things, honestly known by a few, serving simply — that is a life being tilled, and it is precious. A circle of ten doing it together — that is soil forming, of the kind Movement II taught you to look for. But a *congregation* in which circle after circle is quietly doing these things — leadership first, then the hungry hearts, then the friends they invite — that is something else again. That is fallow ground breaking up across a whole field: *"Sow for yourselves righteousness; reap steadfast love; break up your fallow ground, for it is the time to seek the LORD, that he may come and rain righteousness upon you"* (Hosea 10:12, ESV). Notice, one last time, the order in that verse. The breaking of ground is ours to do, and it is commanded before the rain and without a forecast. The rain is His — His timing, His measure, His glory.

None of this requires a movement, a budget, or a platform. It requires the decision to begin, a handful of people to begin with, and the patience to keep at it through ordinary weeks and dry ones. Which brings this movement to its final question — the one everything so far has been preparing you to answer: what would it actually look like for *your* church to run this, on purpose, season by season — leadership first, then the circles, then the congregation? That is the pilot, and it is the next chapter but one. The tools are on the table. The field is outside the window.
