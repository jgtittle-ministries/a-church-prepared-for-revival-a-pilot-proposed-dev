# It Starts at the Top

> **Draft status:** working draft (DEV) — offered for prayer and honest discussion.

Everything in this movement — the tilling practices, the vessel-building, the pilot itself — waits behind a single door, and the door is this chapter. If Movement II found hungry hearts in your congregation, the natural instinct is to go serve them: announce something, launch something, put the hungry into groups and get started. This chapter asks you not to. Not yet. Because there is a first plot of ground that has to be broken before any other plot will take seed, and it is not in the pews.

It is the leadership's own soil.

Here is the conviction this chapter carries, stated as plainly as we can state it: **this has to start with the leadership of the church, who should be a fellowship of the heart already — and whatever it takes for that culture change to happen at the top is the actual beginning of revival preparation.** Not a preliminary to the beginning. The beginning. A church can skip this step; churches do it all the time. What it cannot do is skip this step and still arrive where it meant to go, because a congregation will not go where its leadership has not gone. It never has. There is no recorded exception, and there is a reason there is no recorded exception, which is where we have to start.

## Sheep read shepherds, not memos

Every church runs two curricula at the same time.

The first is the stated one: the sermons, the mission statement, the discipleship pathway on the website, the values painted on the lobby wall. The second is the lived one: what the leaders actually do, what they actually love, what they make time for, what they avoid, what happens to their faces when someone tells them the truth. And in every congregation on earth, when the two curricula disagree, the people learn the second one. Not because they are cynical. Because they are sheep, and sheep read shepherds. It is how God built them.

Jesus said it in one sentence: *"A disciple is not above his teacher, but everyone when he is fully trained will be like his teacher"* (Luke 6:40, ESV). Notice what the verse does not say. It does not say the disciple will be like the teacher's *teaching*. It says the disciple will be like the *teacher* — the person, the actual life, the real interior condition leaking out through a thousand small unguarded moments. Full training does not produce people shaped like your doctrinal statement. It produces people shaped like you.

Peter, writing to elders, gives the same truth from the shepherd's side: shepherd the flock of God that is among you, *"not domineering over those in your charge, but being examples to the flock"* (1 Peter 5:3, ESV). The instrument of leadership Peter names is not authority, though elders have it, and not eloquence, though some have that too. It is *example*. The elder leads by being, visibly, a certain kind of person — which means the elder's own walk with God is not his private business with a public role attached. His walk with God *is* the curriculum. So is yours.

This is why programs alone have never prepared a church for revival and never will. A program is a memo. You can staff it, fund it, announce it beautifully — and the congregation will look straight past it at the leaders and ask the only question sheep ever really ask: *is this real in them?* If the answer is yes, the program almost doesn't matter; the life will spread by contact. If the answer is no, the program almost doesn't matter either.

## Not run one — be one

So what, concretely, is being asked of a leadership team here?

Not that the pastors and elders *administer* a fellowship-of-the-heart initiative. Not that they approve it, resource it, or even champion it from the front. All of that is running a fellowship. The ask is different in kind: that the leadership circle **become** one. That the pastors and elders of the church meet together, regularly and for a long season, as a small group under exactly the same disciplines they will later invite the congregation into — no observer's seat, no facilitator's exemption, nobody in the room on official business.

What does that look like in practice? The working model this book leans on — [*Fellowship of the Heart*](https://jgtittle-ministries.github.io/fellowship-of-the-heart-pilot-at-cca/) — describes it in detail for its own setting, but the disciplines are simple enough to name here, and they are the same at every scale:

**Scripture together — expecting to hear.** Not sermon preparation. Not curriculum review. The leaders open the Word the way they hope their people someday will: reading a passage slowly, asking the living God what He is saying to *them*, in this room, this week — and telling each other honestly what they heard, or that they heard nothing. For many leadership teams this will be the strangest part: men who handle the Word professionally every week, sitting under it together as amateurs again, with nothing to produce.

**Listening prayer together.** Time in the meeting — real time, not the closing thirty seconds — where nobody talks, and the group asks the Father a genuine question and waits. Then each person says what came, if anything came, without embarrassment either way. This is the practice a hearing church is built from, and it cannot be taught from a pulpit by people who do not do it. It can only be caught from people who do.

**Honest confession — starting with dryness and fear.** Here is where the room gets quiet. The discipline is that the leaders tell each other the truth about their own interior lives: the pastor who has not felt God's presence in eight months and preaches anyway; the elder who is afraid the whole thing might not be real; the staff member who is exhausted and has started to resent the sheep. Every leadership team contains these people. Almost no leadership team contains a room where they can say so. Building that room — slowly, carefully, with the confidentiality of a true circle — is the single most important act of soil preparation this book will propose, because a leadership that cannot confess its own dryness to itself will build a church where no one else can either.

**Truth-telling, with the bad-news test applied inside the room.** Movement II gave the whole church a diagnostic: *what happened to the last person who told us something we did not want to hear?* Now aim that question at the leadership circle itself, from the inside. Can an elder tell the senior pastor that a beloved initiative is failing? Can a younger staff member say *the way we made that decision hurt people* — and be thanked for it? Can anyone in the room say *I think we are drifting* without becoming, over the following months, slightly less welcome? The guardrails chapter said that truth traveling upward is the one thing every other instrument misses. The place that habit is formed — or found to be missing — is the leaders' own table.

Notice what all four disciplines have in common: none of them can be delegated, none can be performed, and none produce anything a board report can capture. That is not a design flaw. That is the design.

## What it costs at the top

It would be dishonest to present this as anything but expensive, so let us count the cost out loud.

**It costs the calendar.** A covenanted season of this kind — and we will argue below it must be a season of months — means a standing block of hours that produces no visible output. Nothing gets built in those hours. No decisions get made, no problems get solved, no ministry gets administered. For leaders whose weeks are already spoken for twice over, this will feel, for a while, like waste. It is the opposite of waste — it is the one meeting from which every other meeting will eventually draw its life — but the calendar does not know that yet, and something currently on it will have to die. What a leadership removes from its schedule to make room for God is the first legible signal to the congregation about what the leadership actually believes.

**It costs control.** In every other room the leaders enter, they are the ones holding the agenda. In this room the agenda is held by Someone else, and He has never once submitted His plans for approval. A group that genuinely asks *what are You saying to us?* has surrendered the outcome of its own meeting — and leaders, of all people, hate that. It is also, of all the costs, the one most worth paying, because a leadership that cannot surrender a ninety-minute meeting to God has no business asking Him to take over the whole church.

**It costs the pastor first.** This must be said carefully and it must be said. In any circle like this, someone has to go first into the deep water — has to be the first to confess real dryness, name real fear, tell the group something that costs him — and in a leadership circle, that someone is the senior leader. Not because he is the neediest person in the room, but because he is the most watched. If the pastor shares safely — polished struggles, resolved doubts, weakness from ten years ago — every person in that room will instantly learn the actual rule, and the actual rule will be *we do not really do this here.* The leader who intends to ask a whole congregation to open its heart must open his own first, in front of the people who work for him, which is the most expensive audience he has.

He is not without a model. On the night He was betrayed, the Lord of the church took the lowest place in the room, on His knees with a towel, and then told them why: *"If I then, your Lord and Teacher, have washed your feet, you also ought to wash one another's feet. For I have given you an example, that you also should do just as I have done to you"* (John 13:14–15, ESV). Whatever else the basin means, it means this: in the Kingdom, the one with the most authority goes down first, so that going down becomes safe for everyone else. Vulnerability in a leadership circle works exactly the same way. It flows downhill from the senior chair, or it does not flow.

## The freedom in it

Now the other side of the ledger, because this chapter is not a burden being laid on tired people. There is a door being opened for them.

Consider what the office of pastor or elder quietly does to a soul. It puts a person in front of the congregation as the resident expert on God — the one who explains the Word, voices the prayers, sits with the dying, answers the doubts. And year after year, the role whispers a lie that almost every leader eventually half-believes: *you are the one who has arrived. Your job is to have it together. Your questions are a liability now; your dryness would be a scandal; your fear must never show.* The office does not exempt the soul from the ordinary struggles of faith. It endangers it — because it makes the ordinary struggles unspeakable, and unspeakable struggles do not heal. They go underground, and they metastasize, and every few weeks another name we all know becomes another story we all grieve.

This is why shepherds need shepherding most, not least. Paul's charge to a young pastor puts the order plainly: *"Keep a close watch on yourself and on the teaching"* (1 Timothy 4:16, ESV) — *yourself* first, before the doctrine, because a leader's own soul is the vessel everything else is carried in. And the Psalms describe David's leadership of Israel the same way, interior before technique: *"With upright heart he shepherded them and guided them with his skillful hand"* (Psalm 78:72, ESV). Heart, then hand. There is no version of skillful hands that compensates for an unattended heart — not in a king, not in a pastor, not in an elder board.

So here is the freedom: a leadership circle that becomes a fellowship of the heart is the one room in a leader's life where he gets to stop being the answer and go back to being a pilgrim. Where the pastor can be a sheep for ninety minutes. Where the elder who has been running on doctrine and duty for a decade can say so, and find out that the men around the table are not shocked — they are relieved, because it is true of them too. Leaders who have tasted this describe it the same way almost every time: *I did not realize how alone I had been.* The circle this chapter proposes is not one more obligation for depleted people. For many of them, it will be the first place in years where their own hunger — the hunger that took them into ministry before the ministry buried it — gets fed.

And a leadership that is being fed can invite others to the table without pretending. That is the whole difference between recruitment and witness.

## How a leadership team actually starts

Practically, then. Four counsels.

**Covenant a season — months, not weeks.** Agree together, out loud and in writing if it helps, to meet as this kind of circle for a defined season before anything is announced to the congregation: six months is a sane minimum, a year is not too long. The length matters because of what the circle is for. A four-week experiment produces guarded people doing exercises; trust of the kind this requires — trust sufficient for a pastor to confess dryness in front of his own board — forms on the timescale of seasons, the way soil does. The early weeks will be awkward and will feel unproductive. Expect that; covenant through it. The group that quits in week five was measuring the wrong thing.

**Announce nothing.** During the covenanted season, resist the strong institutional instinct to turn the circle into a story — a sermon illustration, a vision-night teaser, a line in the annual report. The moment the leaders' fellowship becomes content, it becomes performance, and everything in it subtly re-aims itself toward the audience. Meet in obscurity. The congregation will be told, at the right time, and honestly; better yet, the congregation will *notice* — because changed leaders are hard to hide — and what they notice will do more than any announcement could.

**Nothing to perform, everything to receive.** Enter the season with the goals set down. This is the one church initiative whose success cannot be defined in advance, because its whole posture is receptive: the leaders are coming to hear, not to build. If after some months the honest fruit is only this — *we know each other's real condition now, and we have begun to hear Him together, and we are no longer alone* — that is not a modest outcome. That is the foundation of everything else in this book.

**Get help if the culture is stuck — and it may be.** Some leadership teams will read this chapter and know, with sinking clarity, that their current culture cannot do it: too much history, too much politics, a wound in the room nobody names. That knowledge is not disqualification; it is diagnosis, and it is exactly the fallow ground Hosea was pointing at. Whatever it takes for that culture to change — an outside facilitator for the first season, a retreat before the rhythm starts, a hard reconciling conversation that should have happened years ago, in some cases a leader stepping back — *whatever it takes* is not a detour from revival preparation. It is revival preparation, already underway. The plow has simply found the first rock, and the first rock was always going to be ours.

## The warning, given honestly

And now the sentence we would spare you if we could, offered with love and without softening.

A church that skips this step and launches programs anyway will get a result — churches almost always get a result — but the result will not be what the program promised. **It will be a faithful reproduction of the leadership's actual culture, wearing the program's name.** This follows directly from Luke 6:40, and it is confirmed by more church initiatives than any of us can bear to count. If the leaders' real culture is performance, the church will get small groups that perform vulnerability — fluent, warm, and sealed. If the leaders' real culture is control, the hearing-God language will become one more management vocabulary, and the guardrails of Movement II will be breached within two years by people quoting them. If the leaders' real culture is distance — courteous men who have worked side by side for a decade and do not know one another's hearts — then the congregation will learn distance with Scripture verses attached, which is harder to cure than distance alone.

The fully trained disciple will be like his teacher. Not like the curriculum. Like the teacher. A church can print anything it wants on the lobby wall; it will reproduce what is actually in the room where its leaders meet. That is not pessimism. It is simply the reason this chapter comes first in the movement, and the reason there is no route around it.

## The first good soil

Here is where the chapter has been going.

When a leadership circle genuinely becomes a fellowship of the heart — when the pastors and elders of a church are actually meeting under the Word, actually listening together, actually confessing their dryness and telling each other the truth — two things exist in that congregation that did not exist before, and both are made of soil.

The first is the ground itself. Somewhere in the church, the thing this whole book describes is *real*: hearts open to God and to each other, hearing and obeying together, at whatever cost. Small — a dozen people, maybe fewer — but real, the way a first plowed furrow is real. Revival preparation is no longer a proposal in a book. It has an address.

The second is the proof. When the invitation eventually goes out to the congregation — *come, open your heart, learn to hear Him, let yourself be known* — it will not be an invitation to a theory. The people extending it will have lived it, been changed by it, and paid for it, and the congregation will be able to see all three. Sheep read shepherds. Let the first thing they read be this: *our leaders went first.*

That is what it means to start at the top. The rain has not come yet; the field is still mostly unbroken; but there is one plot of good soil now, and it is in the right place — upstream of everything. What the church builds next is the vessel to hold what God may send, and that is the work of the next chapter: [The Vessel Before the Rain](reader.html#docs/movement-iii-preparing-the-soil/02-the-vessel-before-the-rain.md).
